# RDI (Research Driven Inspiration) — Full Synthesis

## What RDI is

**Research Driven Inspiration (RDI)** is a structured framework for building what its authors call a **prepared mind**: a depth of understanding of an industry or space that lets you see where it's heading and act on it before others can. Instead of starting with a solution and trying to validate it, you start with curiosity about a space and let discovery drive you toward conviction — and ultimately toward a hypothesis about the future: what's changing, what's becoming possible, and where the opportunity lives.

The methodology was developed by **Scott Brady and Brett Jordan** and has been taught at Stanford GSB (STRAMGT 546) to hundreds of MBA students exploring industries ranging from healthcare and energy to supply chain and AI. It has produced venture-backed companies, sought-after roles in new industries, and investment theses. The framework has continued to evolve, particularly as AI tools have changed how quickly people can build real depth in a space.

The intellectual heritage is explicit. Louis Pasteur: *"In the fields of observation, chance favors only the prepared mind."* Peter Thiel and Mike Maples' notion of **earned secrets** — insights you can't Google, only discover through deliberate inquiry, real conversations, and sharp synthesis — sits at the core of the approach. By synthesizing across the experiences of thought leaders, stakeholders, and industry experts, RDI's claim is that an outsider can spot gaps, shifts, and second-order effects that even insiders miss.

**The problem it solves.** Capable people get stuck in two ways at the start of an entrepreneurial journey. Some never begin, waiting for a lightbulb moment. Others manufacture one — a brainstorm over a bottle of wine the night before an application is due, or a serial entrepreneur gathering smart friends for a weekend whiteboarding session. Both treat idea generation as an event rather than a process, and both pay for it later in constant pivots, shaky conviction, and years of effort. RDI is the antidote: a system that produces the kind of understanding that would otherwise take years of industry immersion, in months.

**Who it's for.** Anyone with the curiosity and motivation to do the work — founders, would-be founders, career-changers, investors building a thesis, or anyone going deep on something that fascinates them. The output is the input for many paths simultaneously: a company, a role, a thesis, a reputation as a recognized expert.

**Three things that have to be present at once.** RDI works because mindset, strategy, and process reinforce each other.

- **Mindset** — exploration first, not validation. You're not starting with a problem and working toward a solution. You're starting with curiosity about a space. It requires humility and a willingness to engage with strangers.
- **Strategy** — learning efficiently by standing on the shoulders of the people in a space. Industry insiders typically can't do this; they don't talk to competitors, regulators, technologists, or adjacent customers. As an outsider, you can.
- **Process** — turning the above into momentum through 100+ conversations across roles, companies, and vantage points. The most successful teams have well over a hundred.

Remove any one and the system breaks down.

## When in the entrepreneurship journey to use it

RDI sits **upstream of every other decision** an entrepreneur makes. It is the journey's beginning, not its end. It is a **prerequisite** for ideation, customer development, MVP scoping, fundraising, hiring, or any commitment of real resources.

In contrast to methodologies that start with a problem statement or a solution hypothesis (Lean Startup, customer development) and move outward from there, RDI starts earlier: with the recognition that you do not yet know which problem matters, and that the act of figuring that out is itself a structured discipline. By the time you've finished an RDI cycle, you have:

- A defined problem worth solving (or evidence that a particular hypothesis is not worth pursuing)
- Deep expertise in the space
- A network of relationships built through the research itself
- The credibility to recruit, fundraise, or pitch

The framework explicitly **does not force a single outcome**. Its output can lead to building a company, joining one, investing in the space, or continuing to deepen expertise as an advisor or thought leader.

**What to expect on tempo.** Progress arrives in waves, not in a steady linear march: a breakthrough conversation that reframes everything; a week of wandering followed by one that feels like a leap forward. Urgency, the authors say, is the enemy of curiosity. Trust the process.

## The Methodology — Step by Step

RDI is a **three-phase, iterative process**. Phases 1 and 2 are largely sequential setup; Phase 3 is a cyclical engine that runs until you have the conviction to act.

### Phase 1: Team Formation & Alignment

The groundwork. Before research begins, you need to know what you're looking for, who you're looking with, and how you'll work together. Critically: **the team forms before the problem is selected**. This is counterintuitive — people typically want to know what they'll be doing before they pick collaborators — but RDI partners are not co-founders. They are research partners bound by a common interest, shared goals, and a desire to identify non-obvious problems.

**Sub-steps:**

1. **Self-Reflection.** Get clear on your own motivations, working style, strengths, weaknesses, pet peeves, fears, must-haves, show-stoppers. Your professional background does not have to define direction — many successful RDI outcomes come from people exploring industries they had no prior experience in. The combination of existing expertise plus genuine curiosity about a new space often produces the most original insights.
2. **Building Your Team.** Typical team size is 2–3. Highest-performing teams have **diverse and complementary** backgrounds — everyone strong at the same thing is a warning sign. On including a technical partner: depends on the space; deep technical expertise can also lead to tunnel vision. The right mindset matters more than the right skill set.
3. **Going Solo.** Possible and sometimes successful. Pros: speed, simpler decision-making, no communication overhead. Cons: no moral support, no accountability, no early warning for tunnel vision. Solo researchers need to build replacement support systems deliberately.
4. **Internal Alignment.** Transparency upfront about motivations, working styles, commitment levels, time available, communication preferences. Goals don't have to be identical, but they have to be **compatible** (one person wanting a bootstrapped business and another wanting a venture-backed unicorn is a real misalignment). **Shared core values** are non-negotiable; respecting different values is fine, but small misalignments here compound.
5. **Setting Norms.** Working principles paired with specific behaviors. Most important: a regular operating cadence.
6. **Defining Success → the Opportunity Rubric.** A list of criteria for what good looks like at the end. Starts as a high-level filter, evolves into a scorecard with must-haves, nice-to-haves, deal-breakers, red flags. *The act of building the rubric and having the conversations is as valuable as the tool itself.*
7. **Extended Support Systems.** Past teams describe building this support network as one of the highest-value activities in the RDI process. Mix of technical advisors, strategic sounding boards, industry river guides, peer thought partners. These relationships develop organically — an unusually generous interviewee becomes a sounding board; a peer with sharp questions becomes a thought partner.
8. **Getting Organized.** Operating cadence (a mix of short syncs and longer working blocks), tools and shared workspace (Notion, Google Docs, Airtable), and AI as a research companion. Start simple; add tools as the need becomes obvious.

**Phase 1 outputs:** team formed, interest area or shortlist, outlined goals and norms, opportunity rubric.

### Phase 2: Ramp-Up & Bench-Level Analysis

The real learning begins. Phase 2 is primarily **secondary research** — reading, scanning, mapping, absorbing — to become "**cocktail party conversational**" in your space. Not an expert yet, but fluent enough to engage credibly with insiders and earn the right to ask good questions.

**Sub-steps:**

1. **Picking a "Haystack."** The area you select to explore and sift through in search of the proverbial needle. Two filters:
    - **Team fit** — curiosity (not passion — passion builds with effort), how backgrounds and experiences map to the space, and alignment with the team's goals from Phase 1.
    - **Market fit** — evaluated through three lenses:
        - **High-tide markets** (Jim Ellis, Stanford): industries that are growing and accumulating value.
        - **Change vectors** — internal (labor shortages, regulation, consolidation, shifting power dynamics, new standards) and external (AI, IoT, cloud, macroeconomic shifts, demographics, sustainability, geopolitics). The most compelling opportunities often live where an external force collides with an internal reality.
        - **Fragmentation and complexity** — siloed industries where companies each hold one or two pieces of a giant puzzle that no one has assembled.
2. **Dialing In Your Scope.** Optimize your aperture. Survey broadly first ("bare eyes, then binoculars") before narrowing. Plant a flag: "We're exploring X because we believe Y is changing and creating new opportunities." Hold it firmly enough to act on, loosely enough to let learning reshape it.
    - **Warning signs.** Scope is too broad if you can't find experts (you'll find experts in *French cuisine* or *indoor agriculture*, but not in something as vague as *food*). Scope is too narrow if your hypothesis reads like a problem definition or product concept — that means you're past the point where RDI is the right tool.
    - **Scope test questions:** Can you name specific people who would be considered experts in this space? Can you articulate what's changing and for whom? Can you describe distinct segments within it? Is there enough room to explore and be surprised?
3. **Building a Primer.** Not a deliverable. The currency that makes primary research productive. A process, not an event. Objectives: get smart quickly (vocabulary, players, dynamics), stress-test your market hypothesis, confirm your interest, build a foundation for primary research. By the end, you should be conversationally fluent.
4. **AI as a Research Partner.** Used well, AI compresses weeks of reading into days; used badly, it becomes a crutch that produces shallow understanding.

**Phase 2 outputs:** defined focus area and market hypothesis, industry primer, ecosystem map, initial outreach target list, preliminary questions and perspectives.

### Phase 3: Research, Synthesis & Evaluation

The core of RDI. Where the real insight and expertise are earned. Phase 3 is a **cyclical engine** that runs through five iterating activities — outreach, interviews, synthesis, evaluation, refinement — and continues until you have conviction to act.

**The cycle:** identify and reach out to people across the space → conduct interviews that deepen understanding → capture and synthesize what you're learning → use insights to sharpen questions, update hypotheses, and identify the next set of conversations. Each pass builds on the last. Over time, interviews get easier to land, questions get sharper, pattern recognition improves, understanding compounds.

**Sub-steps:**

1. Who to Learn From — identifying interview targets across seven stakeholder categories.
2. Running Your Outreach — strategy, writing, managing the pipeline, alternative channels.
3. Questions That Unlock Real Learning — the philosophy and craft of exploratory questioning.
4. Running Effective Interviews — pre-interview prep, in-conversation discipline, post-interview debrief.
5. Connecting the Dots — synthesis methods (clustering, mapping, 2x2s, stakeholder comparison, hypothesis tracking).
6. Sharing What You're Learning — to interviewees directly, and publicly.
7. Refine, Refocus, Repeat — using the rubric, knowing when to commit, pivot, or kill.
8. Diving Deeper — focused 2-week sprints to pressure-test an emerging opportunity.

**Phase 3 outputs:** deep expertise and fluency, a clear view of the problems and opportunities that matter most, tested hypotheses with conviction, a network of relationships, a foundation for whatever comes next.

## Research Techniques

### Secondary Research (Phase 2: Bench-Level Analysis)

The "bench-level" phase. The rule of thumb: **any question you can answer by Googling is not a good question to ask an expert during the limited time you have their attention.** The primer phase is about harvesting the low-hanging fruit so primary research can focus on what only humans can give you.

Sources, organized by what you're trying to learn:

- **Landscape** — industry reports and databases (IBIS World, CB Insights, EBSCO, ProQuest) for market size, segmentation, growth trends.
- **Players** — D&B Hoovers and Tracxn for incumbents and emerging companies; Pitchbook and Crunchbase for startups and funding; earnings call transcripts and SEC filings as free, rich sources on how public companies think about strategy and risk.
- **Conversation** — trade publications, industry newsletters, podcasts, conference agendas, industry-specific Substacks, Reddit (verify elsewhere) for tone and temperament of the space.
- **Insider perspectives** — Tegus and AlphaSense for expert interview transcripts; Google Scholar and patent databases for technical and research-driven spaces; tech transfer offices and spinouts as signals.

AI tools accelerate the ramp-up but are largely trained on publicly available data. **Paywalled industry reports, expert transcripts, proprietary company data, and academic databases are yours to bring to the table.** If you have institutional access through a university or employer, lean into it while you can.

Also useful: **early low-stakes conversations** with friends, classmates, colleagues. Practice talking about your space, test early understanding, and activate your network so people start thinking of you when they encounter relevant information.

### Primary Research — Who to Learn From

Seven stakeholder categories to target:

1. **Established Players** — incumbents and smaller companies up and down the value chain. The deepest pain points often sit here. Target every level (CEOs, VPs, Directors, Managers, ICs); strategy looks different from execution.
2. **Startups** — founders and early team members. Founders see the macro opportunity; early employees see the granular tradeoffs. *Caveat:* startups are in problem-solving mode. The problems worth identifying are often ones no founder has yet seen.
3. **Investors** — partners, principals, focus-area leads. They've thought deeply about spaces they invest in, know what's being built and what isn't, and can make introductions. **Corporate venture arms** are especially valuable — they sit at the intersection of incumbents and startups.
4. **Experts and Thought Leaders** — authors, journalists, industry advocates, retired insiders, consultants. Broad pattern recognition. **River guides.** Identify through who wrote what, who's quoted, who hosts and guests on podcasts. Expert network platforms (GLG) for hard-to-reach specialists.
5. **Academics** — institutions with named centers, where most-cited papers come from, where startups spin out. Citation count and authorship conventions (first author = lead, last author = senior supervisor) for navigating Google Scholar.
6. **Government, Policy, and Regulatory Bodies** — agency staff, program leads, former regulators. Public hearing records, grant recipient lists, advisory boards, trade associations as identification channels.
7. **Customers and Users** — direct and adjacent.

**Where to start within an organization:** aim *lower* than the C-suite first. People closer to the work are more willing to educate, more tolerant of foundational questions, and more excited to share hard-earned knowledge. *By the time you reach the CEO, you may know things about their company that they don't.*

**Work-backward exercise:** imagine yourself one or two years in the future having identified a problem and committed to it. Who would you need around you? Potential customers, suppliers, competitors, investors, acquirers, advisors, co-founders, early team members. Those are exactly the people you should be reaching out to now.

### Primary Research — Running Your Outreach

**Why people respond to RDI researchers.** Most cold outreach in professional contexts is transactional (someone wants a job, a sale, an intro). RDI outreach is different: you're not asking for something, you're offering something — curiosity, a fresh perspective, and a willingness to come back and share what you learn across the landscape. Put yourself in the recipient's shoes: that's a conversation most people want to have. Protect that dynamic. The moment outreach starts to feel like networking or selling, you lose the thing that makes it work.

**Two approaches to email:**
- **Semi-tailored batched** — for common roles across many companies. Tailored by role and segment.
- **Fully personalized** — for specific, irreplaceable people.

**Boilerplate blasts with zero customization** yield the lowest response rates and are not recommended. Past teams have achieved 20–30% response rates on batched emails and over 90% on highly personalized ones.

**Anatomy of an outreach email:**
- **Sender address.** A `.edu` or institutional address is a huge unlock — carries immediate credibility, dramatically increases open rates.
- **Subject line.** Answer the two questions every recipient has: *who are you, and what do you want?* Effective examples: "[Institution] grad researching grocery inventory management"; "[Institution] alum researching IIoT data management — quick chat?"; "Researching IIoT data management — quick chat? | [Name of reference] ref"
- **Body — five elements:**
    1. **Introduce yourself and your connection to the space** (motivation, why you're exploring, relevant background).
    2. **Explain why them** — what about their experience makes this conversation valuable.
    3. **Find connections** — mutual contacts, referrals, articles they've written, events they've attended, shared background.
    4. **Offer something** — to share your learnings. Mind confidentiality in sensitive industries.
    5. **Clear ask and next steps** — typically a conversation; offer to suggest times or send a calendar link.
- **Before sending:** check the spelling of every name. Confirm titles. Read it aloud. A misspelled name closes the door before you walk through it.
- **Follow up.** A short, well-timed follow-up can double your response rate. Plan for it when you draft the initial outreach.

**Sourcing contact information.**
- Best source: someone you've already connected with (ask whoever referred them).
- Third-party contact-sourcing tools.
- Email syntax inference: find an email format on the company's website (press release, newsroom), search for a common name plus the company domain ("john" + "@xyz.com"), guess common formats (first.last, first_last, firstlast, firstinitiallast, first@), send to all and see what doesn't bounce.
- Conference attendee lists — registering for one often gives access to names, roles, contact info. Past lists can sometimes be found with `filetype:pdf` or `filetype:xlsx` searches.

**Managing the pipeline.** Outreach has to be **constant and parallel** — sourcing, emailing, following up, scheduling, interviewing, and debriefing all happening at once. The good news: it fuels itself. New connections come out of interviews you're already doing. Once momentum builds, teams have nearly stopped cold outreach entirely.

**Tooling.** A lightweight CRM is consistently recommended. At minimum, a shared spreadsheet or Notion table tracking who you've contacted, when, sourcing, response status, and notes. A step up: email tools with templates and tracking. At the high end: full sales outreach platforms. Caveat: increased automation skews transactional. Tools support thoughtfulness; they don't replace it.

**Beyond email.** Three illustrative non-email approaches from past teams:
- A team researching ports hired a taco truck, parked it by the docks, gave away free tacos, and asked workers about their day. Wealth of information; high goodwill.
- A team hosted a virtual conference on their topic at the GSB. Gave insiders a platform, drew speakers eager for an academic audience, demonstrated the team's passion, and produced an attendee list that became further outreach fuel.
- A team publicly published its learnings as it went — articles, summaries, LinkedIn posts, industry magazine pieces. Originally intended as giving back, became their most powerful outreach channel: executives at the largest companies in their space reached out directly, leading to job offers, mentorship, investment.

The common thread: **reduce friction, demonstrate genuine interest, meet people where they already are.**

### Primary Research — Questions That Unlock Real Learning

The purpose of RDI interviews is **not** to collect opinions, validate hypotheses, or extract specific data points. It is to **understand someone's lived experience** within an industry — their concrete stories, the friction they actually encounter, what tends to break under pressure. RDI interviews are forward-from-curiosity, not backward-from-an-answer.

The discipline:

- **Hold hypotheses lightly.** Wanting a hypothesis to be true is natural; trying to make it true, consciously or unconsciously, corrupts learning. Be just as excited about disproving a hypothesis as proving one. Disproving is often the faster path.
- **Avoid sales-call drift.** When research conversations turn into pitches, one of two bad things happens: people get guarded and less forthcoming, or they get *polite* and tell you your idea sounds great. The second is worse — you walk away feeling validated when the signal isn't real.
- **Open-ended questions as the default.** Start with *what, why, how*. Avoid *can, do, are* (closed yes/no). Open-ended questions invite stories, reduce burden, encourage deeper listening, and let the conversation go where the learning is.
- **Probing follow-ups are where the gold lives.** Most of the questions you ask should be responses to what you're hearing in real time: *What led to that? What are the constraints? Tell me more. What usually goes wrong? How would that change how you do things today?*
- **Pressure-test problems.** People are eager to be helpful; they offer problems they think you want. A problem that's real but no one has tried to solve is often a problem no one actually cares enough about. Probe: *Why does that matter? What's the impact? How have you attempted to solve it? Is this your top priority? If it went away, what would that mean?*
- **Broad to narrow flow.** Same logic that guides scoping a space guides structuring a conversation. Start at high level; let themes and priorities emerge; narrow into specifics later.

**RDI-specific question categories** (use as lenses, not a script):

- **Their company and role** — what do they do, how are they measured, what does a typical day look like.
- **Issues and obstacles** — biggest current obstacles, things they find frustrating/cumbersome/outdated, "there's gotta be a better way" moments, things that should be possible but aren't.
- **Risks, fears, and future failures** — premortem framing: "If two years from now your initiative didn't have the expected impact, what happened?" What keeps them up at night. Emerging tech or regulatory threats.
- **Unlocks and enabling factors** — magic-wand questions. What tools exist today and what they're used for.
- **Shifts and disruptions** — what's different now vs. one or two years ago, current headwinds and tailwinds, what's exciting vs. concerning.
- **Best practices** — companies, countries, or industries to look to. Who tackled this well? Will they connect you?

**Know your audience.** A CEO and an engineer need different framings of the same underlying question. Even when entry points differ, the line of inquiry stays aligned — the goal is to test the same hypotheses through complementary perspectives.

**Don't over-script.** Interview guides are guides, not scripts. When conversations become sequences of pre-written questions (or worse, read aloud), they feel like interrogations. Two healthier modes:
- A **deep categorized pool** of open-ended questions you draw from depending on flow.
- A **small set of big themes** (five or fewer) you explore through open-ended and probing questions in the moment.

Both require active listening and full presence.

### Primary Research — Running Effective Interviews

The math: 50–100+ interviews for most RDI projects. If that sounds daunting, don't overthink it — start with the first, then the next. Momentum builds quickly.

**Pre-Interview Preparation.**
- Lightweight background research on the participant. LinkedIn, company website, recent talks or articles. Refresh how they were sourced — if from an article, skim it; if a referral, recall who and why. For follow-up conversations, revisit previous notes.
- Capture context in a scannable format at the top of your notes.
- Treat preparation as a checklist, not an open-ended task. 10–20 minutes per interview.
- Logistics: timing, links, backup contact info, note-taking setup, recording or transcription tools. **Obtain consent before recording** — explicit consent is legally required in many U.S. states.
- **Why recording matters.** Words matter; nuance is lost in paraphrasing. The significance of a remark often doesn't register in the moment, especially early before mental models are formed. Many RDI teams have discovered important insights only after revisiting transcripts weeks later. Recordings also let you compare conversations side by side, where patterns often become obvious.
- **Teaming up.** Early on, two people per interview is valuable: one leads, one notes. The lead can stay present; the note-taker captures detail, nuance, exact language. Different people notice different things, leading to richer debriefs. Have a secondary backchannel (Slack, WhatsApp, paper) to flag threads discreetly. In virtual interviews, non-leading team members can turn cameras off after the start.
- **Be present.** Remove distractions. Develop a pre-interview routine to show up calm, focused, and curious. When you're present, many best practices take care of themselves.

**Conducting the Interview.**
- Interviews are often only 30 minutes. In that window, you're learning, building credibility, building rapport, and laying groundwork for further introductions — simultaneously.
- **Opening.** Conversational tone. Brief context on your research. Demonstrate you've done your homework. Start personal and high-level so the participant warms up.
- **Guiding.** Active listening, **90/10 rule** — listen 90%, speak 10%. Few core themes, no script. Follow energy. Don't fear silence — pauses, especially after a question, often precede the most thoughtful responses.
- **Managing yourself.** Curious, not performative. Don't try to look smart. Avoid interruptions — jot the thought down and return to it. Reflect the participant's language back. *They are the expert in the conversation.*
- **What to listen for** — beyond explicit answers:
    - **Emotions and energy** — frustration, excitement, anxiety, pride. Tone, pacing, body language.
    - **Frames and narratives** — the mental models people use, the rules and constraints they treat as fixed.
    - **Inconsistencies and surprises** — contradictions between what people say and do, or between sources.
    - **Human signals and personal context** — small details that humanize the person and create natural follow-up hooks.
    - **Signals about the interview itself** — engagement level, openness to follow-up.
- **Closing.** Watch the time; check in as you approach the agreed endpoint. Ask for connections naturally ("Who else would you recommend I speak with?"). Offer help in return ("Is there anything I can do that would be useful to you?"). Thank them sincerely.

**Post-Interview Wrap-up and Debrief.**
- 20–30 minutes immediately after, while fresh.
- Reflect: what was obvious, surprising, shocking? Where did emotion show up? Which stories stood out?
- Clean up notes. Key takeaways and open questions at the top. Pull exact quotes where language was precise or revealing. Distinguish facts from interpretation.
- Evaluate the interview itself: what worked, what fell flat, what to adjust next time.
- Debrief with the team. Whoever conducted the interview walks through insights and open questions.
- Housekeeping: update tracker, log follow-ups, send a thoughtful thank-you that references something specific.

## Synthesis Techniques

Synthesis is the bridge between exploration and clarity. As your knowledge base grows, your ability to absorb new information and spot meaningful signals improves. Over time you build the prepared mind.

**What this stage feels like.** Deep into interviews, you may feel *more* confused than when you started. That's a good sign. Your understanding isn't getting worse — it's getting more honest. The neat categories from the primer are getting messier; things you thought you understood have turned out to be more nuanced. The temptation is to either force clarity too early or keep collecting data hoping it resolves itself. Synthesis is the antidote to both.

### How Synthesis Evolves

- **Early synthesis** — organizing and noticing. After the first wave of interviews, sort and tag. What topics keep coming up? What language do people use to describe the same thing? Where do stories overlap or diverge? Keep categories loose; resist conclusions.
- **Mid-stage** — pattern recognition and comparison across stakeholder types, roles, company sizes. How do executives describe a problem versus operators? What do startups see that incumbents don't? Where do multiple independent sources point to the same tension?
- **Late** — testing and pressure-testing. Are your convictions real? Can you articulate why you believe what you believe and what would change your mind? Structured team debate; stress-testing strongest hypotheses against evidence.

### Hands-On Synthesis Methods

The mindset matters more than the specific method: **synthesis is a thinking exercise, not a deliverable.**

- **Pile Building (Input Breakdown).** Convert a single source — interview, article, report — into discrete, tagged scraps you can work with. The act of working with AI to extract scraps is itself how you ingest the material; don't outsource it fully. Build the pile source by source as you gather material. Once a few dozen scraps deep, move to clustering.
- **Cluster Analysis.** Group accumulated scraps into distinct, active claims. Name clusters with active, insight-oriented labels ("Data exists, but no one trusts it"; "Gatekeepers block experimentation"). A vague label ("Data Issues") hides the insight. **Tensions between clusters are signal, not noise** — contradictions point to where the next round of research should go. Re-cluster as more material accumulates.
- **Mapping and Process Diagrams.** Value chains, workflows, regulatory flows. Who depends on whom, where bottlenecks live, where switching costs come from. Tensions in the system (control vs. flexibility, growth vs. margin, centralization vs. decentralization) become visible. Layer in timing, regulation, and technology to see how the system is changing.
- **Attribute Maps and 2x2 Frameworks.** Plot players, products, or customer segments along two dimensions. The first set of axes is rarely the best — the real insight comes on the second or third iteration when a framing makes the landscape snap into focus. AI is especially useful here for trying many combinations quickly and filtering by region, stage, or buyer type to reveal patterns within patterns.
- **Stakeholder Comparison.** Lay perspectives side by side. Executives, operators, investors, regulators, customers describing the same industry through different lenses. Disconnects aren't noise — they're signal. The places where perspectives diverge most sharply often point to the most interesting opportunities: misaligned incentives, unrecognized pain points, blind spots created by where someone sits.
- **Hypothesis Tracking.** A running list of explicit hypotheses with supporting evidence, contradicting evidence, and confidence levels. A shared doc with columns is enough. Review weekly. Some hypotheses gain weight; others collapse. Both are progress. Tracking helps you distinguish a pattern that's real (six independent sources without prompting) from one you want to be real (two enthusiastic interviews and a gut feeling).

These tools work best in combination, alongside written synthesis.

### Writing as Synthesis

Not all synthesis is visual. Writing forces you to confront what you actually know vs. think you know. If you can't explain it clearly, you probably don't understand it as well as you thought.

- **The living primer** — keep updating it through Phase 3. New information slots in or reshapes the structure. Gaps become prompts for additional exploration.
- **Memos and briefs** — concise (1–2 pages), focused write-ups. A memo captures themes from a cluster of interviews or a pattern across stakeholders. A brief is more hypothesis-driven — reframing a problem or making the case for a deep dive. Useful at transition points.
- **White papers and thought pieces** — more time-intensive, more comprehensive, more outward-facing. Build credibility with serious stakeholders.

*These documents don't need to be perfect. They are tools for thinking. If something is hard to write, that's usually a sign you need to think about it more.*

### AI in Synthesis

AI has fundamentally changed what's possible. Try three clustering approaches in the time it used to take to do one. Re-cut a 2x2 with different axes in minutes. Re-segment by stakeholder type or geography. Ask AI to identify themes you might be missing across dozens of interview transcripts. **Stay in the driver's seat** — use AI to prepare inputs, generate alternative groupings, pressure-test conclusions, and extend thinking. But *you* form the perspective. The test: if you can explain what you're seeing and why it matters without referring back to what the AI generated, you've done real synthesis.

## Idea Generation from Research (Ideation)

Synthesis builds understanding. Ideation is what you do with it. Synthesis asks you to be disciplined and evidence-based; ideation asks you to be creative, speculative, willing to explore ideas not yet fully supported. Both are essential, but conflating them produces either premature conclusions (treating a half-formed idea as validated pattern) or stifled creativity (dismissing an idea because you can't yet prove it).

**Capture continuously.** Ideas surface throughout the research process — a pattern noticed during synthesis sparks a product concept; a gap between what two stakeholders described triggers a "what if." Don't filter, don't judge, don't evaluate yet.

**Structured ideation sessions.** Where synthesis sessions ask *what are we seeing?*, ideation sessions ask *so what could we do about it?* and critically: **what will this space need two to three years from now that nobody is building yet?**

Principles for productive ideation:
- **Start from your synthesis.** Use clusters, tensions, stakeholder comparisons, open questions as launchpads. The best ideas are grounded in real patterns, not invented in a vacuum.
- **Think about the future, not just the present.** Imagine what problems will emerge, what tools will be needed, what shifts will create openings.
- **Build on each other's thinking.** "Yes, and…" An idea that seems impractical in its first form may contain a kernel worth developing.
- **Capture everything.** Sort by type (product, user, workflow, infrastructure) or ambition. Don't delete anything.
- **Use AI as a creative partner.** Riff, explore adjacencies, generate variations, push past first instincts into second and third-order implications.

You're not just ideating to "find a startup idea." You're generating possibilities to test, refine, and carry forward. The ideas that emerge become inputs for evaluation against the rubric — where you'll filter, pressure-test, and decide which deserve deeper investigation.

## Sharing — Direct and Public

A core RDI move and one of the highest-leverage activities in the whole framework. Sharing is what transforms one-time conversations into ongoing relationships and what generates inbound momentum.

**Directly with interviewees.** A follow-up that goes beyond "thank you" — reference something specific you learned and offer something in return: a relevant article, a data point from elsewhere in your research, a best practice you heard from someone in a similar role. As research deepens, what you share becomes more valuable — a pattern across their industry they can't see from their vantage point, a draft framework that maps to (or contradicts) their experience.

**Publicly.** Different objectives: build credibility, sharpen thinking through writing for an external audience, generate inbound connections.
- **What to share.** Start by simply putting it in the world that you're exploring this space and why. From there: observations as they develop (interesting findings, surprises, questions you're wrestling with), more structured perspectives (a synthesis of themes, a take on where the market is heading, a framework), eventually a substantive piece (white paper, longform article).
- **Where.** LinkedIn and Substack as accessible starting points. Industry publications and newsletters for credibility. Conferences and events for in-person presence. Industry podcasts.
- **Involve the people who shaped your thinking.** Quote (with permission), tag, acknowledge — expands reach and creates feedback loops.

Publishing creates a tipping point. One past team substantially reduced cold outreach once inbound momentum built up. Writing for an external audience also produces a different kind of rigor than writing for your team.

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating idea generation as an event rather than a process.
- Starting with a solution and searching for validation.
- Scoping too broadly (can't find experts) or too narrowly (you've already decided).
- Tunnel vision from deep technical expertise.
- Leading questions and confirmation bias.
- Research conversations drifting into sales calls.
- Over-scripting interviews.
- Skipping recording / relying on paraphrased notes.
- Treating problems people offer at face value.
- AI as crutch instead of partner.
- Forcing clarity too early in synthesis.
- Endless data-collection hoping it resolves itself.
- Inconsistent rubric application / drift.
- Holding too many options too long.
- Conflating "not yet" with "not ever."
- Avoiding hard conversations within the team.
- Treating sharing as a late-stage activity.
- Letting outreach fall behind the interview cycle.

## RDI vs. Other Methodologies

**vs. Lean Startup / Customer Development.** Lean Startup begins with a falsifiable business-model hypothesis and iterates through Build–Measure–Learn cycles. Customer Development is similar: Steve Blank's four-step model assumes you have a working hypothesis about a customer and a problem and tests it through structured interaction. Both presume that *the problem* is the starting point. RDI explicitly **rejects that starting point.** It is **needfinding for industries, not for individual customers**. The output of RDI is the *input* to Lean Startup or Customer Development. They are sequenced, not competing.

**vs. classic "expert interviews" in consulting or investing.** Those are structured, transactional, designed to support conclusions already forming. RDI interviews are forward-from-curiosity, not backward-from-an-answer.

**vs. Design Thinking / user-centric needfinding.** Design Thinking starts with a defined user or customer and goes deep on their workflow. RDI is system-level: industries, structural forces, change vectors, second-order effects.

**vs. ad hoc industry research.** Without the cyclical structure, without the rubric as a discipline, and without the commitment to 100+ conversations, casual research produces opinions, not earned insight. RDI's distinguishing feature is the **deliberate structure layered onto curiosity**.
