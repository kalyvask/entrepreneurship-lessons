# Design Partners

> The guiding idea: version 1.0 of your product gets finished through the act of delivering a delightful experience to a real early customer, not in isolation before one.

Design partners are early customers who **co-create** with you — testing, criticizing, refining your MVP until it delivers true delight. They are the bridge between customer discovery (Stage 02) and PMF measurement (Stage 06) for B2B startups.

A design partner is **not** a beta tester, **not** a free pilot, and **not** just an early adopter. The distinction is structural: a design partner makes a commitment to invest specific time, give specific feedback, and pay specific money, in exchange for influence over what gets built and a price/terms advantage they can't get later.

Choosing the wrong design partners is one of the most expensive missteps a B2B founder can make. The wrong ones eat your engineering roadmap, take your free pilot, and don't convert. The right ones turn your value hypothesis into a sellable product within months.

## When you need design partners

- B2B SaaS or enterprise tooling
- Sufficient evidence from Stage 02 (Customer Discovery) that a specific segment is desperate
- A draft value hypothesis from Stage 04
- You're about to start building the MVP (Stage 05) and need 2–5 specific customers committed to it

For pure B2C plays, design partners look different (closed alpha groups, paid early access, community founder programs). The Unusual approach is B2B-specific.

## The ideal design partner — four traits

1. **Challengers, not giants.** $20M–$250M in revenue, growing >50% annually. Big enough that their endorsement carries weight; small enough to move fast, take risks, and care about you. Fortune 500s eat your roadmap and won't sign in under 12 months.

2. **High-margin industries.** Tech, media, finance — industries where experimentation budgets exist. In low-margin spaces (e.g., grocery, manufacturing, low-end services), there's no slack for unproven tools, and design-partner mechanics break down.

3. **Urgency measured in days/weeks, not quarters.** *"When can we have it?"* not *"Let's set up a pilot review for Q3."* If their procurement and security review takes 6 months, they're not a design partner — they're a future customer.

4. **Reference value.** Their endorsement will carry weight when you scale. Their CEO's tweet, their case study, their willingness to take a reference call — that's the post-PMF dividend you're paying for now.

## The advocate inside

Every great design partner has **a champion** inside: your *advocate*. Not the company, not the buyer — a specific human whose career-bet is on your product working.

What an advocate sounds like:

- *"We've tried solving this ourselves. We tried everything but haven't been able to get there."*
- *"This is what we've been waiting for — can we go deeper?"*
- *"Tell me when I can show this to my CEO."*

That's desperation language. Inside their organization, this person rallies internal buy-in, defends the budget line, navigates procurement, and makes sure your product survives the political review when it threatens an existing process or vendor.

Without an advocate, the design partner program will fail no matter how good your product is. *Find the advocate first.*

## Design partners vs. early adopters vs. customers

| | Design partner | Early adopter | Customer |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stage | Pre-MVP through early MVP | Early MVP | MVP onward |
| Number | 2–5 | 10–30 | Scaling |
| Relationship | Co-create the product | Use early version, give feedback | Buy and use |
| Commitment | Bi-weekly feedback, ROI commitment, paid | Pay reduced price for early access | Standard purchase |
| Influence over product | High (you build for them, with them) | Some (feature requests, bug reports) | Aggregate signal only |
| Your obligation | Heavy: bi-weekly cadence, Slack, dedicated PM time | Light: product works | Standard: SLA, support |
| Price | Discounted in exchange for partnership | Discount in exchange for early adoption | Full price |
| Goal | Reach MVP-ready, sellable v1 | Validate market beyond design partners | Scale revenue |

A design partner is **not** an early adopter. The relationship is structurally different. You owe them more, they owe you more, and the bilateral commitment is the whole point.

## Qualifying ruthlessly

Before you sign a design partner, qualify them with these questions:

1. **Will they commit to 30–45 minute feedback calls every two weeks?** If they hedge — "maybe," "if I'm available" — they're not a design partner. Real partners block the time.

2. **Have they previously spent money and failed to solve the problem?** A "previous failed attempt" budget is the strongest single signal of desperation. If they've never tried, the pain may be theoretical.

3. **Can they help quantify the ROI of using your product in their day-to-day?** If they can't articulate "this saves us X hours / $Y / Z FTEs," they don't yet have the operational use case clear. Push them; if they still can't, they aren't ready.

4. **Do they have an advocate with the budget authority or path to it?** A junior PM who loves you but can't sign a contract is not a design partner.

5. **Can they decide and move in days/weeks?** If their answer is "let me bring this to the next committee," your design partner program will stall.

If you can't answer **yes to all five**, do not sign them as a design partner. Decline gracefully and circle back later as a normal customer.

## Structuring the relationship

Once qualified, define explicitly:

### Cadence

- **Bi-weekly 30–45 min check-ins.** On the calendar. Both parties block the time.
- **Shared Slack channel** (or Teams equivalent) between your team and theirs. Async questions, screenshots, feedback in flow.
- **Quarterly business review** (after first 3 months): are we on track to the success metrics?

### Success metrics (define together)

- *"By [date], using [product], [customer] will achieve [specific outcome]."*
- Tied to measurable business value (hours saved, revenue captured, errors avoided).
- Both parties agree these are the success criteria — and the criteria for converting from design partner to standard customer.

### Pricing

- Discounted from your eventual price, in exchange for design-partner commitments
- Common patterns:
  - 50–70% off year 1 with auto-renew at full price
  - Free during MVP phase, then commit to paid annual on a specific date
  - Equity-light grant in exchange for letter-of-intent + early commitment
- **Always paid in some form** — even small. Free programs don't filter for desperation; they filter for "curious people with budget for free things."

### Exclusivity (occasionally)

For competitive industries, design partners may want category exclusivity. Be careful:
- Time-bounded (6–12 months max)
- Defined narrowly (their direct competitor list, not their entire industry)
- With clear graduation terms

### IP and feedback rights

- You retain all IP
- You retain the right to incorporate their feedback (it's the point)
- They get attribution opportunities (case studies, references) but not exclusivity over the resulting product

## Mechanics that work

Past patterns that have produced fast PMF for B2B teams:

- **Weekly written status update** (you to them, not them to you) — "here's what we shipped, what's next, what we need from you"
- **Embedded representative** in their Slack — your eng or founder, not a customer success rep
- **Open-book product roadmap** — they see what's being prioritized, can argue for their needs, but the final call is yours
- **Failure exits** — if they want out, agreed-on terms. No legal drama. (Better to lose one cleanly than fight one badly.)
- **Quarterly "would we still buy from us if we were starting fresh today?"** check-in — surfaces drift early

## When to graduate from design partners

You're done with the design-partner phase when:

- 3 of 5 design partners are at full-price standard contracts
- The remaining 2 are committed to convert (date set, terms agreed)
- You can articulate the standard offering without design-partner-specific carve-outs
- New prospects can buy *without* the bi-weekly cadence (i.e., the product stands on its own)

After graduation, design partners become **anchor customers** — your reference accounts, your case studies, your land-and-expand seeds.

Some founders try to keep the design-partner discount in perpetuity to retain the relationship. **Don't.** A long-term unsustainable price corrupts your unit economics and trains the customer to expect concessions you can't give to scale.

## The three whys (a related discipline)

Every design-partner conversation should answer three questions explicitly:

1. **Why buy anything?** Why is this problem urgent enough to allocate budget? (Often: a specific event triggered it — new regulation, new competitor move, new internal initiative.)
2. **Why buy now?** Why not next quarter? Next year? What's changing?
3. **Why buy from you?** Why your product over the alternatives — including DIY, doing nothing, hiring a person?

If you can't get clear answers to all three from a design-partner candidate, they aren't actually desperate. Walk away.

## Common pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why it kills you |
|---|---|
| Designating any early customer as a "design partner" | Without commitment, you're giving away influence for nothing |
| Free pilots with no conversion path | Filters for tire-kickers; doesn't validate willingness to pay |
| Big-logo design partners | Slow procurement, no urgency, eat your roadmap |
| Single design partner | Their idiosyncratic needs become your product — bad for PMF |
| Too many design partners (>6) | You can't service the bi-weekly cadence; each gets diluted attention |
| Building every requested feature | You become a custom-dev shop for their org, not a product |
| Discounted forever | Trains them to expect concessions; corrupts unit econ |
| No champion identified | When the budget tightens, you have no internal defender |
| Mismatched success metrics | They define success differently from you; you'll think you've delivered, they'll think you haven't |
| Skipping the qualification questions | "They seemed excited" — excited isn't desperate |

## Two design-partner failure modes

**The Soft Failure.** A design partner converts on time, on the right terms, but they're so customized to your product they become a low-leverage anchor: every new feature you build is informed by their use case. Your roadmap is theirs. You ship for them, not the market. Symptoms: your second design partner needs heavy customization to match what the first one has; your third churns.

Fix: hold a hard line on what's productized vs. what's custom. *"This is in roadmap; we'll get to it. This is custom; we won't build it but we'll work around it."*

**The Hard Failure.** A design partner doesn't convert. The reasons matter:

- They didn't have the budget — *qualification failure on your part*
- The advocate left — *concentration risk*
- The use case changed — *strategy at their org shifted*
- The product didn't deliver — *honest signal, take seriously*

The right move with a hard failure: graceful exit, document the lesson, don't burn the bridge. A failed design partner can still become a regular customer 12 months later when your product is mature.
