# Customer Interview Playbook

A step-by-step playbook for running a customer interview that produces honest data. Distilled from Steve Blank's customer development, Rob Fitzpatrick's *Mom Test*, and the RDI methodology.

**Use this for:** customer discovery (Stage 02), problem-solution-fit interviews (Stage 03), RDI primary research (Stage 00), and pivot-diagnosis conversations (Stage 07).

## Before the interview (10–20 min prep)

### Background research

- LinkedIn: their role, tenure, prior roles
- Company website: their group, recent news
- Any articles, podcasts, posts they've published
- If referred: re-read who referred them and why

### Capture context

Write at the top of your notes file:

```
[Date] [Person] [Company] [Role]
Background: [2-3 lines]
How sourced: [referral / cold / event]
Why this person: [what makes their experience valuable]
Hypothesis to test: [what you're trying to learn]
```

### Logistics

- Calendar invite confirmed
- Video call link tested
- Recording tool ready (with consent)
- Transcription set up (Read.ai or equivalent)
- Note-taking doc open
- Phone on silent

### Consent for recording

**Required in many U.S. states.** Ask at the start: *"Mind if I record this? It helps me listen better and capture exact language rather than paraphrase. The recording stays with me and I'll send you a copy if you'd like."*

### If interviewing with a partner

- One leads, one notes
- Pre-decide who's primary
- Backup channel for the note-taker to flag threads (Slack, paper, WhatsApp)
- In video calls, non-leading participants can turn cameras off after intro

## During the interview (30 min standard)

### The 90/10 rule

Listen 90%. Speak 10%. **Don't fill silences.** Pauses after questions often precede the most thoughtful responses.

### The five-act structure

**Act 1 — Welcome (2 min)**
Conversational tone. Thank them. Brief context on what you're researching. Don't pitch — describe what space you're exploring and that you're here to learn.

> *"Thanks for taking the time. I'm researching how mid-market vertical farms handle production planning. Trying to understand the real workflow — not from outside, but from people actually doing it. I'm not selling anything. Just want to learn."*

**Act 2 — Their context (5 min)**
Open with broad, easy questions about them and their work. They warm up. You confirm context.

- *"Tell me about your role."*
- *"What does a typical week look like?"*
- *"How are you measured?"*

**Act 3 — The terrain (15 min)**
The bulk of the conversation. Pull from your question categories:

- Issues and obstacles: *"What's been the most frustrating thing recently?"*
- Past behavior: *"Walk me through the last time [X] happened."*
- Workarounds: *"How do you handle that today?"*
- Magic wand: *"If you had unlimited resources, what would you change?"*
- What's different now: *"What's changed in this space in the last year?"*

Follow energy. When they get specific, dig deeper. When they get generic, ask for an example. When emotion shows up, probe.

**Act 4 — Probe and confirm (5 min)**
Specific follow-ups to deepen what surfaced earlier. Pressure-test problems:

- *"Why does that matter?"*
- *"What was the cost the last time?"*
- *"Is this your top priority right now? What's above it?"*
- *"How have you tried to solve this in the past?"*

**Act 5 — Close (3 min)**
Watch the time. Wrap deliberately:

- *"Who else should I be talking to?"* (always ask)
- *"Is there anything I should have asked that I didn't?"*
- *"Is there anything I can do that would be useful to you?"* (offer back)
- *"Mind if I follow up if I have a question that surfaces later?"*
- Thank sincerely.

### What to listen for — beyond the words

- **Emotion and energy** — frustration, excitement, anxiety. Tone, pacing.
- **Frames and narratives** — the mental models they use, the rules they treat as fixed.
- **Inconsistencies and surprises** — contradictions between what they say and what they describe doing.
- **Human signals** — small personal details that humanize and create natural follow-up hooks.
- **What they bring up unprompted** — that's where the pain is alive.

### What to avoid

- **Pitching.** Don't describe your idea. Even when asked, redirect: *"Happy to share later — but first, your perspective."*
- **Leading questions.** *"Would you use a tool that..."* — never. *"How are you currently handling..."* — yes.
- **Closed-ended questions.** *"Do you have X problem?"* gives a yes/no, not a story.
- **Filling silences.** When they pause, wait. Silence is generative.
- **Validation-seeking.** *"Does that sound useful?"* corrupts the rest of the conversation.
- **Compliment fishing.** If they're complimenting your idea, you're pitching.
- **Future-tense questions.** Stated future intent is unreliable. Convert every future-tense question to a past-tense behavioral one.

### When the conversation goes wrong

Symptoms:
- You're talking more than them
- They're complimenting your idea
- They're suggesting features
- You leave feeling validated

Fix in the moment:
- Redirect to a past behavior question
- *"Tell me about the last time this came up."*
- *"How did you handle it?"*
- *"Walk me through what you did."*

## Immediately after the interview (20–30 min, while fresh)

This is the highest-value 30 minutes of the interview process. Most teams skip it.

### Surface impressions

Without referring to notes, ask yourself:
- What surprised me?
- Where did emotion show up?
- Which stories will I remember?
- What's the one thing that was *different* from my prior conversations?

Write these down first.

### Clean up notes

- Key takeaways at the top
- Open questions at the top
- Pull **exact quotes** where language was precise or revealing
- Distinguish **facts** (what they did/said) from **interpretation** (what you think it means)

### Evaluate the interview itself

- What worked?
- What fell flat?
- Where did I lead? (Be honest.)
- What would I change next time?

### Score the desperation markers

For each customer, mark:
- [ ] Would pay with little proof
- [ ] Has tried to build / hack / work around the problem
- [ ] Mentioned pain unprompted
- [ ] Wants it now

3+ markers = desperate. 1–2 = needy (do not build for them yet). 0 = polite.

### Action items

- Send thank-you within 24 hours, referencing something specific
- Update interview tracker
- Schedule the next interviews from intros they offered
- Park the follow-ups they offered (recording link, article, intro)
- Add new hypothesis or pattern to your synthesis doc

## Cross-interview synthesis

After every 5–10 interviews:

1. Re-read all your notes from the batch
2. Cluster: what patterns recur?
3. Pressure-test: where do interviews contradict each other? *(Contradictions are signal — they point to different segments or different hypothesis branches.)*
4. Update the desperation scorecard across the batch
5. Update your hypothesis tracker

See [`playbooks/synthesis.md`](synthesis.md) for the full synthesis method.

## Common mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| No prep | 10–20 min minimum |
| Pitching | Replace every "we're building..." with "tell me about how you currently..." |
| Talking too much | 90/10 rule. Use a timer if needed. |
| No recording | Get consent and record. Words matter. |
| Skipping the debrief | The debrief is half the value |
| Counting compliments as validation | Compliments are zero signal |
| Only talking to people who say yes | Talk to people who pushed back too |
| Stopping at 5 interviews | Depth shows up at 20–60 |
