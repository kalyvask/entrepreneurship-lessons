# Lean Launchpad Source Synthesis

Comprehensive notes from Stanford ENG 245 ("Lean Launchpad") — Winter 2025 quarter. Sources: the 2025 syllabus, the Testing Business Ideas reading guide, ten lecture decks (Weeks 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 8, 10 plus the Week 1 interview guide deck, the Week 2 Where-to-Focus deck, and Glenn Kramon's cold email workshop), Jeff Epstein's "What Investors Want" deck, and the Week 10 final-presentations brief.

## Overview and Philosophy

Lean Launchpad simulates a startup. The class explicitly is "essentially a lab" — books and lectures are tools, not answers. The work happens **outside the building**, talking to ~120 customers, partners, and suppliers over ten weeks. Teams "cannot delegate customer discovery" — every member interviews.

Core methodology, taught explicitly in Week 1:

- **Lean Startup**: come in with a set of untested hypotheses, validate them quickly by getting out of the building. Use the Business Model Canvas to diagram value creation and validate hypotheses toward a repeatable, scalable business model.
- **Get Out of the Building**: customer development — run weekly experiments to test assumptions about features, pricing, distribution.
- **Agile Development**: iterate rapidly. Build MVPs weekly. Avoid hypotheticals. Get real customer feedback. Use it to iterate (small adjustments) or pivot (substantive changes).
- **Pivot**: substantive change to your business model hypotheses, made *before* spending years and millions. Can be as small as changing position in the value chain or as large as rethinking the core technology.

Teaching style — "Relentlessly Direct" — adapted from Radical Candor (care personally + challenge directly). Critiques happen in front of peers, weekly. Three unexcused absences = grounds for failure.

Expectations per team per week: 10–12 customer interviews (~120 total over the quarter), a weekly Lessons Learned presentation, MVP iteration, mentor check-in, and documentation in Notion. First MVP due by Week 3 (Jan 21 in the 2025 calendar).

Grading: 15% individual class participation, 20% out-of-building progress, 20% homeworks, 20% weekly team presentations, 25% final presentation. Multiplied by a peer-grading multiplier.

Texts: *Testing Business Ideas* (Bland and Osterwalder), *The Mom Test* (Fitzpatrick), *The Startup Owner's Manual* (Blank and Dorf). Recommended: *Talking to Humans* (Constable and Rimalovski), *Slide:ology* (Nancy Duarte).

The two myths the class hammers from Week 1:

1. **"Brilliant ideas suddenly happen."** Andy Rachleff: "You often stumble into product-market fit. Serendipity plays a role, but the process to get to serendipity is incredibly consistent."
2. **"Raising seed money = completed customer development."** The "typical" VC-backed startup is great pedigree → great pitch → big round → hire fast → launch to no demand → burn cash → fail. The "successful" version is unique insight from years in a space + your own problem + extensive research + experiments first, *then* raise to execute.

## The Business Model Canvas

The course uses the BMC as the artifact for capturing hypotheses. Lecture 2 frames the nine blocks against three meta-questions:

- **Desirability** — Do customers want this? (Customer Segments, Value Propositions, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams)
- **Feasibility** — Can I build and distribute it? (Key Activities, Key Resources, Key Partners)
- **Viability** — Will it make money? (Revenue Streams vs. Cost Structure)

Failure modes by quadrant (each illustrated with a real company in the deck):

- **Not desirable** — Quibi ($1.75B raised, 6-month life) "didn't plan on testing these hypotheses. With fewer than 10 employees but almost $2B in the bank, it plans on jumping right in."
- **Too desirable but not feasible** — Washio promised 24-hour laundry turnaround, learned too late that 48 hours (even a week) was fine, but had already burnt the cash.
- **Not feasible** — Zume Pizza ($446M raised) couldn't keep cheese from sliding off pizzas in moving trucks; technical issues plus food-safety regulation killed it. Jawbone ($900M raised, $3B valuation, dead) — reliability problems forced no-questions refunds within a month of launch.
- **Not viable** — Webvan ($800M, IPO, dead) — wrong segmentation, overly complex infrastructure, expanded too fast.

The example BMC the class uses to teach the layout is Filed (LLP '21), which restated its full value prop in one canonical sentence: "For B2B sales reps who waste time with documentation gruntwork, Filed is a tool that makes it easy for sales reps to quickly document and share information. Unlike our competitors, we automatically organize and push information to other tools like Salesforce and Slack."

### Week 1 — Business Model and Customer Development (Jan 6-8)

**Pre-class**: Watch Steve Blank's videos #7–#42 of the Lean Launchpad Playlist (Customer Development and the Business Model Canvas, ~1 hr); Customer Interviews #1–#3 of the LIFFFT Playlist (~20 min); read *Startup Owner's Manual* chapters 1–3 (Customer Development and Discovery intro); read *The Mom Test* chapters 1–3, 5, 8 plus cheatsheet (~1 hr); begin *Testing Business Ideas*.

**Homework**: build an interview guide.

**Why startups fail (CB Insights teaching frame)** — most fail at the *first* step: customer discovery and validation. Four ways teams get it wrong, each shown with a case:

1. **Build something "I think" people want.** Quibi vs. TikTok: Quibi's 5–10 minute mobile-format episodes were a $1.75B bet on a hypothesis no one tested. TikTok hit 1.7B MAUs because the format was customer-led.
2. **Build something "cool" instead of something that addresses a need.** Mayfield Robotics (Kuri) won CES, TED, SXSW, the Aspen Ideas Festival, and the Economist Innovation Summit — and folded. iRobot's founder explains it: "Our mentality was 'of course everybody wants robots in their homes.' But people buy it as an appliance. They buy it as a cleaning device." Same lesson with Lighthouse — "best AI camera" per Gizmodo, "smartest home camera ever" per CNBC — shut down 2018.
3. **Build something that showcases how amazing some tech is.** Same Lighthouse pattern.
4. **Do customer discovery but ignore the signals — confirmation bias.** Richard Feynman: "I never think 'this is what I like' — I think 'this is what it is.'"

**The user-savant concept** — your initial objective is *not* validation but discovery. Become a "user savant" by (1) solving your own problem (Stripe — accepting payments online was stupidly complicated; Zum/LLP '14 — Ritu Narayan needed help transporting her kids; FreeWill — Jenny and Patrick had worked in nonprofits), (2) working years in the space, (3) knowing someone with the problem very well, or (4) doing a lot of market and customer research: spending a day with the user, becoming the user, interviews, reading, video.

**The scientific method for hypotheses** — frame every hypothesis as:

> "I believe that [AUDIENCE] has [PROBLEM] and would pay good money for a solution because [REASONING]."

For consumers, "has problem" can be replaced with "is eager to adopt / desires / wants." Be *very specific* about the audience. Don't assume all users want the same thing — Pebble shipped 2M watches by 2014 but missed its 2015 forecast because it tried to expand beyond geeky hackers into productivity and fitness, "in hindsight, stupid and obvious and 100% my fault."

**How to learn about customers** — interviews, "a day in the life of," surveys, reading, going where customers congregate (Reddit, Facebook, LinkedIn, niche groups like Palo Alto moms), and synthetic audiences. For *consumer* startups specifically, one-on-one interviews are noisier; complement with larger data sets (surveys, online tests). The Bay IVF team (LLP '23) mapped the journey across front desk nurses, embryologist, care nurse, lab director, and the only doctor — discovered that the "EMR" was an Excel macro built in-house.

**Interview tactics — The Mom Test rules:**

- User interviews are about *discovery*, not selling.
- *Find* the pain, don't *force* people to express a pain they don't have.
- Avoid leading questions ("would" questions like "How much would you pay for this?", "Would you use this?", "Do you think this is a good idea?"). They prompt specific responses and rarely produce learning.
- Ask probing questions (the why/how questions). They dig into a user's psyche and uncover what they actually think. "A power user who willingly and honestly answers lots of why questions is worth 10 lukewarm customers."
- The Mom Test do/don't: DO "Walk me through the last time you had to deal with this problem"; DON'T "We're building a tool to help you connect with friends. Is this something you would like?"

**The interview grid** — three sections:

1. *Is there a gap in the market?* — pains, goals, workarounds, metrics, feelings
2. *Is there a market in the gap?* — purchase process, discovery/marketing, comparables, competitors
3. *Follow-up and branch* — ask for follow-up, ask for introductions

**Interview hygiene** — bring "Mom Test" reminders to the top of every guide. Ask to record. Set context (name, role, why you're calling). Post-interview, ask explicitly to stay in touch and for intros. Use transcribers (Otter, Fireflies, Zoom built-in, Sybill, Clarity). Even ask GenAI to summarize — less biased than your own notes.

**Open vs. closed questions** (from the Interview Guides deck): DO "Tell me about the last time...", "Talk me through your day...", "What are your thoughts on X?", "Describe your experience using X?", "How would you improve X?" Always ask probing why/how follow-ups *after* an open question — don't lead from the start.

**Interview math for the week** — aim for 12+ customer meetings per week (30 minutes each). Assume 3 warm intros and 9 cold reach-outs. Conversion rate < 10%, so target 90+ reach-outs (LinkedIn connects, cold emails, group posts). Due to scheduling, some interviews land 2+ weeks after reach-out.

**LinkedIn tactic**: find target. Check if your network has a connection — if yes, ask for a warm intro. If no, *write an email rather than a LinkedIn message* (lower conversion on LinkedIn). Use email-checker.net/validate to find the address.

**You should still talk to consumers** even though their answers are noisier — to understand motivations, day-in-the-life, aspirations. Freudian motivation theory, the seven deadly sins (pride, greed, wrath, envy, lust, gluttony, sloth) as a cheat code for consumer behavior.

#### The HEAD method for experiments

> **H**ypothesis → **E**xperiment → **A**nalyze → **D**ecide

Identify and prioritize hypothesis → design and run experiments → analyze evidence and gain insight → decide: persevere or pivot.

#### What makes a good hypothesis

- **Testable** — you can determine true/false clearly. "I believe 10% of surveyed teachers will say they will buy AI tools annually" (good) vs. "teachers will purchase AI tools that make their jobs easier" (bad — untestable).
- **Precise** — clearly defined who, what, when. "Parents with income above $150k will pay 50% more for tutors recommended by their kids' teachers than recommended by the app" (good) vs. "parents prefer tutors recommended by their kids' teachers" (bad).
- **Discrete** — testing one thing. "ELL who use the site for more than 20 minutes will invite at least two friends within a week" (good) vs. "ELL will use our product daily AND recommend it" (bad).

#### What makes a good experiment

- **Strong evidence** — clearly refuses or accepts a hypothesis.
- **Fast and cheap** — doable, allows more experiments per week.
- **High fidelity** — results reliably guide the real enterprise.

You're on a Pareto curve between effort and quality of evidence — maximize value per effort. Ask: "Could the experiment be simpler?" In customer discovery, the right innovation metric is the *number and quality* of experiments (experiments per week, interviews, MVPs, hypotheses tested, cost of learning).

#### Where to Focus (Jeff Epstein's parallel deck)

Two strategic questions every team faces in weeks 1–4:

1. *Build a product that solves many problems for many customers, OR focus and build a product that solves one problem for one customer?*
2. *Serve all customers and solve all their problems — all at once, or sequentially over time?*

The class answers both with the same principle: **focus.** What's our goal? Product-market fit. We have zero customers; we want one. Getting the first customer is incredibly valuable. We can get from one customer to many customers later.

**Fastest way to your first customer** — a 2x2 of *Value to Customer* (Low/High) by *Ease of Building* (Easy/Hard). Pursue the high-value, easy-to-build wedge.

**Worked examples of phased expansion**:

- **AirBnB**: renting airbeds in the living room of an apartment while the owner is present → room in apartment with owner present → entire apartment when owner is away → room in home with owner present → entire home with owner away → large vacation homes.
- **Facebook**: Harvard (Feb 2004) → Ivy League + Stanford (Mar 2004) → all US and Canada colleges (2004–2005) → all US high schools (Sep 2005) → everyone (Sep 2006).
- **Apple**: Steve Jobs rejoins (1997) → iPod (2001) → iPhone (2007) → iPad (2010) → Apple Watch (2015).
- **Amazon**: choose the first ecommerce vertical from a long list (arts & crafts, automotive parts, baby products, beauty, books, electronics, fashion, food, hardware, health, home, jewelry, kitchenware, luxury, pets, sports, toys). Build a framework: standard part numbers, simple supply chain, large variety, low unit cost, low cost to pack and ship, high demand from customers far from physical stores, potential for valuable user reviews. Apply: books wins on every dimension (+ + + + + + +).

**The focus method** in five steps:

1. Create a long list of possible customer segments and the problems for each.
2. Create a framework: what criteria will we use to select the best segment/problem to focus on first?
3. Apply the framework to your market.
4. Weeks 1–3 — learn about the *best 3–4* segments/problems. Talk to users, build MVPs, run experiments to confirm value prop.
5. About Week 3–4 — select the best *single* focus area.

#### The Value Proposition Canvas (the spine of Week 3)

Two halves — **customer profile** (right side, a circle) and **value map** (left side, a square).

**Customer profile** (what to listen for in interviews):

- **Jobs to be Done** — the *task* they're trying to perform, the *problem* they're trying to solve, the *need* they're trying to satisfy.
- **Pains** — bad outcomes, risks they face, obstacles they're trying to avoid.
- **Gains** — expected gains, improvements they want, what could make their efforts more useful, features they'd want from an offering.

**Value map** (what your product does):

- **Products / Services** — describe how your offering meets the customer's needs.
- **Pain Relievers** — how your offering *alleviates* the customer's pains.
- **Gain Creators** — how your product/service *creates* gains.

**Prioritization** — what value prop means *most*? Biggest job? Biggest pain alleviated? Biggest gain realized?

**Worked example of a paired exercise**: a team building reef outplanting and maintenance service for resort hotels. Customer jobs = keeping occupancy high, keeping guests happy, differentiating from other resorts. Pains = decreased hotel occupancy due to loss of reefs, potential bankruptcy, storm damage. Gains = booked months in advance, ability to charge premium rates, reputation as Ecotourism destination. Products = reef outplanting and maintenance service, within 2 years 50% restored reefs, reef planting as a guest activity, coral mini nursery tours. Pain relievers = outplanting with fast-growing diverse heat-resilient microfragments. Gain creators = long-term contracts to continue to maintain the reefs.

#### Why me, why now?

New technology, exponentially better value prop. Rule of thumb: better by an order of magnitude compared to the entrenched incumbent. (Example: iPod = lots of songs + portability, made viable by the Toshiba disk.)

#### Categories and market types

The class teaches four market-strategy archetypes:

1. **Existing market, exponentially better tech** — Google entering search dominated by Yahoo, AltaVista, Ask Jeeves, InfoSeek.
2. **Existing market, segment dominance** — Southwest in airlines (luxury carriers + hub-and-spoke; Southwest = point-to-point, low-cost segment).
3. **Existing market clone + special knowledge / localization** — Google entering China search (2000).
4. **New category** — Groupon (built daily deals; built their own channel; created the category).
5. **New market entirely** — Airbnb (redefined hospitality, unknown market size, customers don't exist yet).

When to create a new category: unmet customer need, existing categories don't match well, need to differentiate. How to create a new category: be clear on the value prop, *educate* your customers, show how you solve the problem in a way existing categories don't, story telling, build an ecosystem around it.

Most founders want to sell their product to everyone. Large market on day one is *very hard* — not enough money, not enough people, diffused value prop. Start with a smaller market. Decide whether you need a new category. Find the *focused customer segment* with the biggest pain. Map against feasibility and market potential. Dominate that segment, then grow in concentric circles.

Worked positioning examples:

- **Amazon** — new category: online books → cust. segment: people who shop online → VP: huge inventory, anytime/anywhere, cheaper → added VP: reviews, personalization → music and electronics → everything.
- **TaskRabbit** — new market (on-demand labor for everything) → too hard to use, complicated bidding model → specialized services *created* new categories (IKEA pickup and buildout).
- **Google Glass** — everyone, all uses (failure) → focused customer segment: doctors in operating theaters → market segment: services that require real-time, contextual, hands-free AR in the field.
- **WeatherBill** — weather insurance for all → weather technology company → customized total weather insurance → sold to Monsanto for ~$900M.

#### What PMF actually is

The class definition (via Lenny's Newsletter): "A *highly desirable product*, for which you can *find and keep customers sustainably*, while *making a profit delivering the product at scale*."

Andy Rachleff (Wealthfront, ex-Benchmark) on two market shapes:

- **Consumer**: "PMF is organic exponential growth."
- **Enterprise**: "At the end of your free trial, *pull* the trial. If the customer doesn't scream, you don't have PMF. Because if they aren't going to buy at the end of 30 days, they aren't desperate. And if they aren't desperate, you don't have PMF."

#### Fake signals vs. real signals

**Fake**: proofs-of-concept (especially free); selling at negative margin (customer not willing to pay); love from one key person at a company (no one else cares); fast growth *without* retention; large round of financing or investor love; big team because you have the money.

Two case studies of fake PMF:

- **Clubhouse** — Andreessen Horowitz Series C in early 2021 at $4B valuation; 17M MAUs by June 2021; six months later lost 80% of users. Growth without retention.
- **Six Degrees** — excitement about being first is not PMF.

**Real** (Andreessen's "The Only Thing That Matters"): customers buying the product as fast as you can make it; money piling up in your checking account; hiring sales and CS as fast as you can; customers coming to you because they've heard of you; customers expanding usage *within* their companies; users inviting friends; product achieves "cult" status.

#### PMF signs along the journey

- *Pay before product is ready* (Drew Houston on Dropbox: "a visceral sense of your thing taking on a life of its own and lurching forward, like getting pressed into the back of your seat by a fast car or a plane taking off. The standout moment was our demo video hitting the top of Digg and then Reddit.")
- *Wow factor* (Google 1997-1998 — product love at first sight)
- *OK using MVP* (need is so big the MVP suffices — DoorDash 2013, Bozzy LLP '20, Aaptiv 2015)
- *Customer love / cult* (Superhuman testimonials — "moved me physically and emotionally," "saved my marriage — I shall sacrifice my first-born-child for the cause," "I lived in a world void of happiness — since I met Superhuman I can now hear color." Sybill's "Wall of Love.")
- *Easy exponential growth*

#### The Superhuman PMF survey (the course's canonical PMF test)

> "How would you feel if you could no longer use [Product]?"
> a) Very disappointed
> b) Somewhat disappointed
> c) Not disappointed
> d) N/A — I no longer use [Product]

Minimum sample size: **100**. Goal: **40%+ "Very Disappointed."** Then *segment* — focus on the group with the highest %. Superhuman's PMF journey: measure → segment (Founders, Managers, Business Development, Executives) → survey the "somewhat disappointed" → execute on what would push them to "very."

#### Net Promoter Score test

"How likely is it that you would recommend X to a friend or colleague?" Promoters = 9-10. Passives = 7-8. Detractors = 0-6. NPS = %Promoters − %Detractors. Range −100 to +100. Goal: **NPS > 50**.

#### Retention — the quantitative spine

Top 10 apps have ~60% 90-day retention. You can have PMF with less than 60% (e.g., w-commerce, smaller audience). Quick Ratio = Added MRR / Lost MRR > 4 — a "quality of growth" metric.

#### Virality math

- Lost Users = Active Users × churn
- K-factor = number of new invites per customer × conversion per invite
- New Users = Active Users × K-factor
- Active Users(n+1) = Active Users × (1 + K-factor − churn)

Goal: k-factor > churn.

#### How VCs measure PMF — quality of growth vs. growth

| Metric | Good | Better | Best |
|---|---|---|---|
| Growth (CARR %) | 50-100% | 100-200% | 200%+ |
| Retain users (monthly churn) | 1-3% | <1% | Net negative |
| Grow efficiently (CAC payback, months) | 24 | 12-24 | <12 |
| Cash-flow efficiency | <1 | 1 | >1 |

Source: Bessemer.

SaaS Capital valuation formula (private SaaS): VM = -3.2 + (0.32 × SCI) + (8.26 × ARR Growth Rate) + (2.62 × NRR Rate). Worked example: SCI 9.1, ARR $5.0M, growth 40%, NRR 105% → 5.77x multiple.

#### Finding PMF is a process, not a moment

Median time from idea to PMF: **2 years** (Lenny's Newsletter). Initially focus on product (customer validation); then shift to GTM engine (customer creation). DoorDash's Series A Sequoia memo emphasized *sticky customers*.

#### Pivots are a normal part of reaching PMF

Three pivot archetypes:

- **Refocus customer segment** — Superhuman pivoted from "all users" to founders and BD execs. Focusing customer segments is not natural for founders.
- **Refocus value prop / product** — Burbn (everything) → Instagram (photos only).
- **180 change / start over** — Slack came out of a failed game studio.

**Worked LLP pivot cases**:

- **Filed (LLP '21)**: Week 0 → tool for sales to search information. Week 1 → discovered sales reps hate *documenting* (time-consuming, poor UX, same info in multiple tools); pivoted. Week 2 → built BMC around B2B enterprise sales leadership and ops. Week 3 → tested by *selling a competitor's tool* (the sales leader at Productboard: "If I had to get rid of any of our tools, this would be the first to go"). Week 4 → back to the drawing board, found a "hair on fire" problem (sales leader at Atlassian: "We need a common sales model that's not fragmented between B2B and B2C"). Key lesson: **use interviews to guide pivots.** Week 5 → PLG pivot, prioritized leads and lead insights for revenue teams at PLG companies.
- **Rightfoot (LLP '19)**: Week 1 → job marketplace for students to earn money for college. Hypothesis crumbled: 84% of students wouldn't take new work ("I'm not worried, I think my parents will help me pay"); 1/8 parents thought college savings plans were urgent. Pivoted to an app for parents (529 roundups). Then to Bamboo, an employee benefit to pay down student debt — "I'd commit to Thumbtack for 5 years if they paid off my student debt," but 4 of 5 employers expressed concern about program cost. Then to fintech APIs for paying student debt.
- **Verix (LLP '18)**: Week 1 → Betterment for SMBs. Week 2 feedback: COO of a $50M New England distributor: "We're not really interested in any reworking of our financial processes for what amounts to small dollars of incremental interest." Week 5 → loans for SMBs. Discovery: managing cash was not the issue; verifying income/employment was the bottleneck — "I have four analysts sitting in our Palo Alto office who just do spreading. That's the bottleneck." Week 10 → loan processing for banks. By 2020 → employment and income verification.
- **Theia Medical (LLP '18)**: Week 1 → automated on-the-go cataract screening device. 18 doctors said the need wasn't *cataract*: "Cataracts need surgery not when they are present but when they are symptomatic"; "Cataract patients don't need to come back often. Would be great if you could refer DR, AMD patients"; "Cataracts alone are low value — value is $30–40 per referral"; "PCPs don't get reimbursed for cataract screening." Week 10 → onsite optometry clinic "on wheels." 2019 → onsite employee vision benefits. 2020 → employee vision benefits (COVID killed office space; the *benefit* was the durable thing).

Pivots are painful — "like a grieving process for the original idea." Keeping morale high is difficult, and there are only so many pivots a team can take (inversely correlated with team size).

#### A/B testing — taught with case studies

- **Tim Ferriss titled *The 4-Hour Workweek*** by testing 6 titles ("Broadband and White Sand," "Millionaire Chameleon," etc.) with $200 of Google AdWords, bidding on keywords ("401k", "language learning"), and printed high-quality fake book covers placed over other books at Borders Palo Alto while he watched with a coffee.

Result: 433% CTR improvement, $250M+ win in 2014, $204M+ in 2015. Lesson: "You are never done testing. Don't be afraid to call the baby ugly."

#### Why have a financial model at all?

Startup financial models will not be accurate. But:

- *Can my business make money?*
- *How large can it be?*
- *What are my key financial hypotheses?*

The deck opens with two cognitive-bias examples — guess the weight of a male African elephant; which weighs more between two things. The trap is **precision bias** — confusing accuracy with precision; believing greater precision implies greater accuracy.

#### The Shutterstock worked example (the canonical one-page model)

Founded 2003 by Jon Oringer. Sells images to businesses. Hypotheses for the 8th year (2011):

- 500k customers × $2/image × 100 images/year = **$100M revenue/year**
- After photographer payments + web hosting, keep 60% of revenue (gross margin)
- Number of employees
- Operating expenses = Employees + General & Administrative + Advertising
- Operating expenses per employee

**Two types of costs**:
- *Cost of Revenue* (a.k.a. COGS) — manufacturing and delivery
- *Operating Expense* (a.k.a. operating cost) — R&D, S&M, G&A

**Other financial-hypothesis structures**:
- Viewers × Page Views/Viewer = Page Views; × Advertising CPM = Revenue.
- # Units Sold × Revenue/Unit = Revenue.

#### Benchmark

Revenue per employee target: **$150k** (per 2023 KeyBanc Capital Markets and Sapphire Ventures SaaS Survey).

#### The cautionary tale — RentLingo (LLP 2012)

Mission: trusted data for the $10B apartment rental industry, sourced from local experts, residents, and public info. Value prop: "Yelp for Apartments." Channel: distribute through Apartments.com. Exceptional early results — 3× page visits, 5× page views/visit, 5× leads.

Year 3 hypotheses vs. reality:

| | Original | Actual |
|---|---|---|
| Visitors/month | 250,000 | 300,000 |
| Conversion rate | 10% | 5% |
| Sell-through rate | 100% | 60% |
| Leads sold | 300,000 | 108,000 |
| Revenue/lead | $10 | $3 |
| Lead revenue | $3,000,000 | $324,000 |
| Ad revenue | $0 | $180,000 |
| **Total revenue** | **$3,000,000** | **$504,000** |

Raised $570k in 2013; returned $718k in 2020 → 1.3x over 7 years, 3% IRR. The model wasn't wrong on the *first* line (visitors). It was wrong on conversion (50% of expectation), sell-through (60% of expectation), and revenue/lead (30% of expectation). Each plausible-looking assumption was off by half, and the errors compounded.

## Cold Email Tactics (Glenn Kramon's Workshop)

Kramon was a New York Times editor for >25 years (10 Pulitzers, 25 Pulitzer finalists). Won the GSB MBA '20 Distinguished Teaching Award. His Lean Launchpad workshop is the source for cold-outreach craft.

**The goal**: show you're special. **Be 1 *in* a million, not 1 *of* a million.** Make the recipient feel like 1 in a million.

**The two qualities to convey**: **Competence and Warmth.** Example sentence that signals both at once: "By the time I left Nest.com I had saved the company $2 million a year, redesigned the packaging and shipping strategy, and interacted with more exotic animals at the company picnic petting zoo than any other employee."

### The 10 Rules

1. **Know something about the person you're writing, and exploit it.** Research the company *and* the people. Search the web and LinkedIn. Example: a cold email to Kroger CEO Rodney McMullen worked because the team found that he started as a part-time stock clerk at Kroger 42 years ago. The line that opened the revised email: "Because you started as a part-time stock clerk at Kroger 42 years ago, you know how working people who lost their jobs to the pandemic must feel." The team also leveraged that he became COO during the last financial crisis and is close to 60, so probably knew people who endured the Great Depression. (Same approach worked at Albertsons; the relationship led to a $20M gift card distribution partnership.) Counter-example: a generic Stanford Impact Fund email to a Tanzanian maggot-factory CEO didn't work. The revision worked by leading with "Elbow deep in larvae" (his own phrase from a TED talk) and signing off "Elbow deep in cash."

2. **Clear, personal subject line, indicating urgency.** NO: "A possible investment." YES: "Can we work together on a deal?" Warm subject lines from real student emails: "From a Fellow GSBer in a Sea of Whartonians"; "Coffee with a fellow Bay Area Davidson alum?"; "A Colombian in Silicon Valley trying to help a Gringo in Colombia"; "Our Supply Chain Insights from Rotterdam"; "Your advice for a punk-loving investor"; "I have a dream: Our first scholar in elected office"; "Coworker saw me on Tinder — yikes!"

3. **Start fast. Get to the point quickly, not at the end.** **BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front.**

4. **Begin with something they don't know, not with something they do know.** Avoid: "Dear Mr. Bezos: You have transformed industry after industry by changing how people interact with and receive traditional services, whether it be books or the retail experience." Replace with: "Dear Mr. Bezos: I can help Alexa help patients needing personalized follow-up care. A Stanford MD/MBA, I would greatly appreciate a few minutes to discuss how voice-enabled platforms can transform how we deliver healthcare." Don't open with "Hope you are well" — use it as a sign-off if at all. Better sign-off: "All the best" or, NYT style, "Allbest." If you must open warmly during a crisis: "I hope you are testing negative and staying positive."

5. **Name someone you know whom the recipient also knows (and respects).** "Irv Grousbeck suggested I contact you." Or, better, have that someone introduce you. The class story: "everyone has a Bryce Love" (Stanford football star) — find the one shared person and you're in.

6. **Try to compare yourself to the person you're writing.** "Like you, I decided my strength was in startups, not mainstream businesses." But — use a comparison that *few* can make, not a comparison anyone can make. Bad: "Like you, I believe that AI will transform our futures." Good (from a real letter to George Osborne): "Like you, I most enjoy the mesh of strategy, policy, and politics — maybe it is the Magdalen historian in us." Real student example to Robert Smith (the African-American billionaire PE founder): "Like you, I've decided I will build a business in an industry where people like us are underrepresented; I also exhibit the same grit and intensity you've demonstrated throughout your life. You wouldn't take no for an answer when you applied for your first internship and I wouldn't take no for an answer when I decided I would compete in two Division 1 sports as a student athlete."

7. **Can you tell a story you know will make that person smile, even laugh?** Real student to George Osborne after a Stanford visit: "I enclose a photo, in which, as usual, the Americans have better hair than us." The P.S. can warm it up: "P.S. Thanks for your Tweets; few places offer both the latest news in transportation tech and reliably adorable cat content." (To a blogger.) "PS When I was a kid, I wanted to become a professional soccer player as much as you wanted to be a cricket player. Perhaps we can someday watch a Sounders FC match together." (To Satya Nadella.)

8. **Keep it short. Make sure there's an "ask" or next step — and make the ask as small as possible.** NO: "I know that's a big ask, especially given your heavy travel schedule, so saying 'no' is a perfectly fine answer." NO: "I know how busy you are, and understand if you can't make the time. In case you are free, my schedule is highly flexible and I would be happy to follow up with an assistant to find a 20-minute spot if that is easier." NO: "I don't know if you remember me but…" Each of those un-sells the ask.

9. **Offer something in return.** "We have a specialist who can help you crunch that climate data — may I introduce you?" "We're organizing our first climate-tech summit at Stanford. Interested?" "I might have some research you might think worthwhile."

10. **Check it for accuracy, then check it again.** Sheryl Samberg → Sheryl Sandberg. Mark Andreesen, Sequoia → Marc Andreessen, Andreessen Horowitz.

### Try, try again — and be creative

A Slack rep wrote four emails to a Synchrony bank exec over weeks. The fourth used Cameo to commission a personalized video from "everybody's favorite Telenova star, Rogelio de la Vega." Subject: "Special message for [name] from Rogelio de la Vega." Within hours: "Julia, This was hilarious… How did you know I was a fan of Rogelio? Due to your incredible creativity, I will be happy to meet with you. This was a great comic break in a difficult day. My dad passed away yesterday, so this was very welcome comic relief for my entire family."

### On ChatGPT

"Using ChatGPT is like driving a Tesla. If you're not paying attention you will pay the price." It makes you one *of* a million. To be one *in* a million you have to improve on what it gives you — by being more concise and by adding warmth and personality.

## Value Proposition Canvas (Week 3 deep dive)

(Already covered substantially in Week 3 above — repeated here as the user requested a dedicated section.)

The Value Proposition Canvas has two halves and six blocks total. The *customer profile* (a circle on the right) breaks the customer into:

- **Jobs to be done** — the tasks they're trying to perform, the problems they're trying to solve, the needs they're trying to satisfy.
- **Pains** — bad outcomes that are possible, risks they face, obstacles they're trying to avoid.
- **Gains** — improvements they want, what could make their efforts more useful, features they'd want from an offering.

The *value map* (a square on the left) breaks your product into:

- **Products and Services** — what you offer.
- **Pain Relievers** — how your offering alleviates the customer's pains.
- **Gain Creators** — how your offering creates gains.

The exercise in class — pair up two teams, each team works on their ideal customer profile and value-prop canvas alone for 5 minutes, then 2 minutes each walking the other team through it.

A team finds *fit* when their pain relievers actually relieve their customer's top pains and their gain creators actually create their customer's top gains. The class repeatedly returns to the prioritization question: *what value prop means the most to your customer?* What jobs matter the most? What pain could be biggest? What gain could be biggest?

**Common multi-stakeholder mistakes**: confusing org with person (treating "Acme Inc." as the customer rather than identifying the human who decides); single-datapoint extrapolation (one good interview becomes your entire model).

**The "day in the life of" framing** drives most of the value-prop work. Understand the entire ecosystem. For multi-stakeholder products you need a value prop for *each* stakeholder. The water-distribution example: rolling out in 7 countries in 6 months required a value prop for the drivers, the front office, and the affiliate owner — each different.

## Financial Model Structure (Week 8 deep dive)

(Already covered in Week 8 above — repeated for dedicated section.)

**The mindset**: startup financial models will not be accurate. The purpose is not to forecast revenue precisely; the purpose is to expose your *key financial hypotheses* in a form where you can spot the assumption that breaks the business.

**Beware precision bias** — confusing accuracy with precision; assuming a precise-looking number is a true number.

**The one-page model** uses the Shutterstock structure (per the Jeff Epstein lecture):

1. **Revenue formula** — # of customers × price × frequency = revenue. Or viewers × page views/viewer × CPM = revenue. Or units sold × revenue/unit = revenue. The form depends on the business model.
2. **Gross margin** — what % of revenue can you keep after Cost of Revenue (a.k.a. COGS — manufacturing, delivery, payments to suppliers like Shutterstock's photographers).
3. **Employee count.**
4. **Operating expenses** — R&D + S&M + G&A. Often expressed as operating expense *per employee*.
5. **Operating profit** — gross profit minus operating expenses.

Benchmark: revenue per employee **$150k** for SaaS (2023 KeyBanc + Sapphire SaaS Survey).

The course teaches the model through a *failure*, not a success — RentLingo (LLP 2012). Each assumption looked plausible. Visitors were actually 20% *better* than expected (300k vs. 250k). But conversion was 50% of expected, sell-through was 60% of expected, and revenue/lead was 30% of expected. The errors compounded: actual revenue was $504k vs. forecast $3M (1/6th). Return: 1.3× over 7 years, 3% IRR.

The takeaway the class draws is to **test the assumption that, if wrong by half, breaks the business** — usually conversion rate, ARPU, or CAC payback.

## What Investors Want (Week 9, Jeff Epstein deck deep dive)

Jeff Epstein is operating partner at Bessemer Venture Partners, former CFO at Oracle. The deck is dense; below is the full structure.

### How VCs specialize

VCs specialize by three dimensions:

- **Risk Stage** — pre-seed, seed, Series A/B, Series C/D, Series E+.
- **Market Sector** — health, finance, education, retail, hospitality, engineering, military, etc.
- **Geography**.

### The VC market (Pitchbook, NVCA Q4 2023, full year 2023, in $M)

| Stage | # Investments | Median Raised | Median Valuation | $ Raised (Bil) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-Seed | 700 | $0.6 | $6 | $1 |
| Seed | 3,000 | $3 | $12 | $14 |
| Series A & B | 5,400 | $5 | $38 | $40 |
| Series C & D | 4,300 | $6 | $50 | $80 |
| Series E+ | 800 | $13 | $144 | $36 |
| **Total** | **14,200** | | | **$171** |

VCs invest ~$170B/year in the US.

### The power law

5% of invested dollars generate **60%** of all returns.

### Why VC is effective

VC is **"gated capital."** VCs give founders 1–2 years' worth of capital, just enough to get to the next gate. If founders don't add value or reduce risk, VCs will not fund the next round and founders are forced to: cut costs to break even, sell, or close.

**At every funding stage, valuation goes up and risk goes down.**

Two illustrations:

- **Not VC**: Quibi raised $1B Seed (2018) + $750M Series A (2020), launched 2020, out of business 2020. $1.75B in.
- **VC**: DoorDash raised $5.84B from pre-seed (2013, $2M pre-money) through IPO (2020, $29B pre-money). Public Aug 2024: $44B.
- **VC**: Tesla raised $724M from Series A (2004, $15M pre-money) through IPO (2010, $1.3B pre-money). Public Aug 2024: $700B.

### The stage-by-stage milestone map (the spine of the deck)

| Stage | Milestone |
|---|---|
| Pre-Seed | Build product |
| Seed | Prove product-market fit |
| Series A | Prove revenue model |
| Series B | Prove net renewal model |
| Series C and later | Grow to $100M in gross profit |
| Sale or IPO | Expand product line, go global, acquisitions |

The principle: **raise this round to achieve the next milestone.**

### Pre-seed: build your first prototype before raising

DoorDash's pre-seed-stage MVP — duration ~6 months, MVP was a static website, marketing was paper flyers handed out on University Avenue, customers sent text orders to the founders, founders drove to the restaurant, picked up the food, drove to the customer. Team: 4 founders. Cash spent: minimal. Then raised $120k pre-seed from YC.

**Build your product to prove you can build it** — Genentech's startup goal was to create insulin using genetic engineering (very hard). Approach: invest $100k to create *somatostatin* (a simpler protein with 14 amino acids vs. insulin's 51) as proof that genetic engineering could work.

### Seed: acquire paying customers, prove PMF

Paul Graham: "It's better to have 100 people who love you than a million people who like you."

DoorDash at Seed — 6 months after pre-seed. MVP: online ordering, Palo Alto + Menlo Park delivery, ~20 drivers, 2 customer support. Traction: restaurants loved/recommended the product; some users bought several times/week. Team: 4 founders. Cash spent: ~$100k.

**Proof of PMF by business type**:
- **B2B**: 10+ paying, referenceable customers.
- **Consumer (Paid)**: 1,000+ paying, referenceable customers.
- **Consumer (Free)**: 100,000+ Monthly Active Users.

**Superhuman PMF survey** (40%+ "Very Disappointed"; minimum sample size 100) — same test as Week 4.

**NPS > 50**.

**Cohort analysis** — track active users over months and look for retention floors.

**Customers show their love publicly** — Superhuman testimonials.

**Lower your risk with successful experiments, even if they don't scale** — AirBnB founders flew to NY and personally took high-quality photos of apartments for early hosts, because data showed high-quality photos increased conversion. Conversion went up.

### Series A: prove the revenue model

DoorDash at Series A — 9 months after seed. Traction: first region (Palo Alto) growing, metrics improving; second region (San Jose) similar pattern. Team: ~20 FT, key Ops and Engineering hires made, >300 drivers. Cash spent: <$2.3M.

**Prove revenue model — B2B**: Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) payback < 2 years AND 75%+ of salespeople achieving quota.

**Prove revenue model — Consumer**: 2× CAC payback < 18 months.

### Series B: prove net renewal model

**Customer success — prove customers are coming back**: Net Renewal Rate > 100%.
- Net Renewal Rate = (Starting Revenue − Churn + Upsell) / Starting Revenue > 100%

### Series C and beyond: $1B+ valuation goal

For large VCs, **valuation goal is over $1 billion** (the unicorn target).

**As a founder, what are you trying to prove to the VC?**

1. **Prove your market is large enough.** Unicorn and IPO metric: $100M annual gross profit in Years 6–8. For SaaS: # customers × revenue per customer. For Marketplaces: # buyers × # transactions/buyer/year × revenue/transaction × gross margin %.

2. **Prove you can grow fast.** Annual revenue growth > 100%. *Why over 100%?* Because the goal is $100M annual gross profit in 6–8 years.

3. **The ideal growth rate — Triple Triple Double Double Double (TTDDD)**, from Neeraj Agrawal at Battery Ventures:
   - Years 1-3: $0–2M
   - Year 4: Triple, $2–6M
   - Year 5: Triple, $6–18M
   - Year 6: Double, $18–36M
   - Year 7: Double, $36–72M
   - Year 8: Double, $72–144M

   The best companies do TTDDD — including HashiCorp, Twilio, Auth0, Toast, Canva, SendGrid, LinkedIn, Zapier, PagerDuty, Truebill, Cloudinary, Procore, Wix, Shopify, LaunchDarkly, ServiceTitan, Cornerstone (Cornerstone took ~13 years).

4. **5 Ways to Build a $100M Business** (Christoph Janz framework):
   - Elephants: ~10 customers × $10M ARPA
   - Deer: ~100 × $1M
   - Mice: ~1,000 × $100k
   - Squirrels: ~10,000 × $10k
   - Flies: ~100,000+ × $1k
   - (Approximate from the deck chart — both axes are logarithmic.)

5. **Prove you can grow at low incremental cost** — Gross Margin > 75%.

6. **Prove you're growing with little capital** — Cash Conversion Score = ARR / Total Capital > 100%; AND New ARR / Cash Burn > 100% (Bessemer).

7. **Prove your leadership team is complete** — all key roles in place for 12 months with proven execution expertise.

8. **Prove your customers, partners, and suppliers are diversified** — no single one accounts for more than 1% of revenue.

9. **Why now?** Uber started soon after smartphones were widely used by passengers and drivers.

10. **Why you?** Marc Benioff built Salesforce for salespeople like himself.

11. **Show a technical advantage** — Google's PageRank delivered superior search.

12. **Show an economic advantage** — Amazon's low-cost obsession and aggressive exploitation of scale. Bezos: "Your margin is my opportunity."

13. **Show network effects that grow stronger with scale** — every new customer adds value for suppliers, every new supplier adds value for customers (Uber's flywheel).

14. **At later stages, achieve efficiency score of 40+ — "Rule of 40 / Rule of X"** = Revenue Growth Rate + Free Cash Flow Margin. Bessemer's data on public SaaS efficiency over the IPO journey: Prior to IPO 113%, IPO-3Y 73%, IPO-2Y 56%, IPO-1Y 53%, IPO 45%, IPO+1Y 38%, IPO+2Y 35%, IPO+3Y 32%.

## Storytelling for Entrepreneurs (Week 10, George John lecture / final-presentations brief)

George John gives the storytelling lecture. The course's framework for the final presentation is captured in the "Lessons Learned Presentation" brief and the related guidance.

### The goal of the final presentation

"Sell us and yourselves on **how much you've learned**, not the success of your project. We should be thinking 'wow I'm amazed at how much they learned,' not 'wow I want to invest.' (Spoiler: if you aim for the former you hit the latter.)"

Two purposes: inspire and teach future entrepreneurs; hone the entrepreneurial and human art of storytelling.

### The shape of the talk

**Opening**:
- Brief, clear, plainspoken introduction.
- Ultra-brief team-member introductions — one strength each.
- A *narrative hook* — a reason to care.
- "Initial concept was X, final company is Y. Here's the story…"

**Narrative**:
- Flowing human storytelling.
- Next-step decisions in business framed as a *human struggle*, not just an intellectual one.
- "Imagine you're watching this movie — do you care about the characters? Are you rooting for them?"
- **"And then" vs. "but / therefore."** Strings of "and then" are bad. Use "but" and "therefore" to drive cause and tension. (This is the South Park rule: any story made of "and then" is boring; the good stories alternate "but" and "therefore.")

### Six questions to answer

1. Why did we initially want to devote 3 months of our lives to this project? *Make it personal.*
2. What did we learn?
3. How did we learn it?
4. Who did we learn it from? Talk about the people we met along the way. *Make it personal.*
5. *Use numbers for credibility* (the Ogilvy / Jobs rule) — how many people did we talk to? How many people did we interact with?
6. Tell the highs and lows of the story. What were we feeling that week? Excited? Disappointed?

### Humility and reality, in multiple doses

Stories that illustrate the journey: doors slammed in faces, founder strife, insane buying processes or other legacy nonsense, surprising hypotheses you had to reject, reflection on your early-class performance vs. now.

### Pride / excitement / triumph

Stories of achievement, creative ideas, sudden realizations, surprisingly fruitful interviews. *Balance self-effacement and humility with winning.*

### A story of serendipity / epiphany

An interviewee who jumps at your product. A key interview that changes your point-of-view and MVP into what you are today. A lucky break. How did the heroes *feel*?

### Future plans and reflection

- **Today**: optimistic but honest evaluation of product-market fit.
- **Tomorrow**: team members' plans to continue.
- **Looking back**: the intangible learnings from Lean Launchpad. Skills learned. Status of learning goals.

### Tactical recommendations

- Have the Business Model Canvas in the background — enlarge and highlight one key point per slide. Use multiple slides if you need to make multiple points.
- **Big readable fonts.** If you need to show a lot of text, zoom into the parts that represent the most important points or key changes.
- **Concrete language vs. "word soup" / yogababble.** Read it out loud.
- **Don't bury the lede.** Bad slide title: "Week 3." Better: "Our Customers are 23-Year-Old Media Planners."
- Consider writing **full-sentence slide titles**. Reading all the sentences in order should tell the complete story. One idea per slide. The body of each slide is an image, chart, data, quotation — *supporting* the title, not duplicating it.
- Include one page on expected annual revenue, X × Y = Z. Example: 10k business customers × 5 users/customer = 50k users × $1k/year/user = $50M annual revenue.
- Trial run with a blunt friend or AI.
- Have fun.

Bountiful (10-min B2B + 2-min); FreeWill (10-min two-sided B2B/B2C + 2-min); Owlet (10-min consumer electronics); Dermalytics (2-min on team dynamics); Exodus (2-min from Hacking 4 Diplomacy); Aqualink (2-min from Hacking 4 Defense).

## Testing Business Ideas (the Bland and Osterwalder textbook)

The course's reading guide treats this as the *spine* of the experimentation curriculum. The text is divided into a "Design" front matter and a long catalog of experiments. The class splits the reading across four weeks:

- **Week 1 reading**: *Design team and shape the idea* (pages 8-23) — covers who should be on a discovery team, how to frame the initial idea, and how to size the unknowns. Plus *Test cycle: Hypothesis → Experiment → Learn → Decide → Manage* (pages 30-87). This is the iteration loop the class adopts wholesale (rebranded as HEAD in lecture).

- **Week 2 reading**: *Experiments — Select an experiment* (pages 91-100). This is the meta-chapter that explains how to *choose* the right experiment from the catalog. The selection criteria are: hypothesis type (desirability, feasibility, viability), evidence strength, fidelity, cost, setup time, and run time.

- **Week 3 reading**: *Discovery experiments* (pages 101-230). These are early-stage, low-cost, low-fidelity experiments to expose what customers actually want. The course catalog of 10 experiment archetypes maps onto Bland and Osterwalder's discovery section: landing pages, paper testing, advertising, simple surveys, promotional material, customer interviews, "boomerang" (testing whether customers will return), data sheet, search-trend analysis.

- **Week 4 reading**: *Validation experiments* (pages 231-309). Higher-cost, higher-fidelity experiments to confirm what discovery revealed: concierge, Wizard of Oz, pre-sales, single-feature MVP, click-dummy, pop-up store, simulated sale, mash-up, life-sized prototype, letter of intent, A/B test in production. Validation experiments push toward customers spending real money or making real commitments.

The Bland/Osterwalder method's three principles, as the class teaches them:

1. **Use evidence-based decision-making.** Don't trust opinions — gather evidence and let it drive persevere/pivot decisions.
2. **Reduce risk and uncertainty.** Every experiment exists to lower a specific risk on the BMC. Identify the riskiest assumption and design the cheapest experiment that gives strong evidence on it.
3. **Run experiments fast and cheap.** A single high-fidelity experiment is usually worse than three quick low-fidelity ones, because of compound learning. The Pareto curve of experiment effort to evidence quality favors many simple experiments early.

The Test Card structure (Hypothesis / Experiment / Metrics / Criteria) is from the book; the class uses it as the homework template. The Learning Card (also from Strategyzer) captures what you learned, what your insight is, and what action you'll take next. Together they form a closed-loop weekly artifact.

## Class culture and anti-patterns

The course explicitly warns about a set of failure modes that show up every quarter:

- **Confirmation bias** — doing customer discovery but ignoring signals because you've fallen in love with the answer.
- **Building something cool instead of useful.**
- **Building something that showcases tech instead of solving a need.**
- **Treating raising money as PMF.**
- **Org-vs-person confusion** — treating a company name as the customer instead of the human who decides.
- **Single-datapoint extrapolation** — one good interview becomes your entire model.
- **Vanity metrics** — proofs-of-concept (especially free), negative-margin sales, love from one key person at a company, fast growth without retention, big team because you have the money, large round of financing.
- **Leading questions** — "would you buy this?" type questions.
- **Failure to focus** — trying to serve all customers and all problems at once.
- **Failure to pivot when the data says to** — by Week 4 you should know whether you have signal or not. If not, pivot.
- **Building too much** — Quibi spent $1.75B on a hypothesis no one tested. Washio promised 24-hour turnaround when 48 would have worked. Webvan went IPO and died.
- **Skipping the prototype before the raise** — Genentech proved somatostatin before insulin. DoorDash had 4 founders driving food themselves before raising $120k.
