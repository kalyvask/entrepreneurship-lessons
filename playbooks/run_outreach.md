# Outreach Playbook

How to source contacts, write outreach, manage pipeline, and turn a research project into a 100-conversation engine.

## The outreach engine

Outreach has to be **constant and parallel.** Sourcing, emailing, following up, scheduling, interviewing, and debriefing all happening simultaneously. The good news: once momentum builds, it fuels itself — each interview produces 1–3 introductions to others.

A working weekly cadence for RDI / customer discovery:

- ~5–10 new contacts sourced
- ~5–10 follow-ups sent
- ~3–5 interviews completed
- ~3–5 debriefs done
- ~3–5 thank-you / follow-up notes sent

The goal: in 8–10 weeks, 50–100 interviews completed.

## Sourcing — seven stakeholder categories

For RDI especially, target across all seven (see [`frameworks/rdi.md`](../frameworks/rdi.md)):

1. **Established players** — operators at every level. Aim *lower* than C-suite first.
2. **Startups** — founders + early team
3. **Investors** — partners, principals, corporate venture
4. **Experts and thought leaders** — authors, journalists, retired insiders
5. **Academics** — institutions with named centers
6. **Government, policy, regulatory** — agency staff, former regulators
7. **Customers and users** — direct and adjacent

Don't skip categories. Each surfaces different patterns. Investors and academics often produce the best "river guide" connections.

## Sourcing tactics

### LinkedIn search

Best for: established players, startups, investors

```
Filters:
- Title: [specific titles, e.g., "VP of Operations" "Director of Production"]
- Industry: [specific, e.g., "Vertical Farming" "Controlled Environment Agriculture"]
- Company size: [for narrowing to mid-market]
- Connections: 1st and 2nd (to find warm intro paths)
- Posted recently: "X has posted about [topic]" — high-engagement signal
```

### Trade publications and conferences

Authors and quoted experts in industry pubs = high-signal contacts. Conference attendee lists are gold:

```
"[conference name]" filetype:pdf
"[conference name]" "attendee" filetype:xlsx
site:[conference url]
```

### Academic research

Google Scholar for most-cited papers in your space. The lead author (first listed) is the doer; the senior author (last listed) is the supervisor. Tech transfer offices for spinout signals.

### Substack / industry newsletters

Subscribe to 5–10 newsletters in your space. The authors and frequent commenters become contacts.

### Podcast guests

Find 3–5 podcasts in the space. Guests are high-value (they have a strong opinion and they're willing to share it). Email them: *"I heard you on [podcast] — particularly struck by your point about X. Doing some research in this space. Could I get 30 minutes?"*

### Government / regulatory sources

- Public hearing records
- Grant recipient lists
- Advisory boards
- Trade association rosters
- Standards body documentation

### Referrals (the highest-yield source)

The best source of new contacts is people you've already talked to.

In every closing conversation: *"Who else should I be talking to? Mind making an intro?"*

A referral with a specific introduction has 90%+ response rates.

## Sourcing email addresses

When you have a name but not an email:

### Method 1: Reference path
The person who referred them is the highest-yield source. Ask: "what's the best way to reach them?"

### Method 2: Company website
Often a "media inquiries" or "press" contact reveals the email format. Then guess.

### Method 3: Email syntax inference
Try formats:
- `firstname.lastname@company.com`
- `firstname_lastname@company.com`
- `firstinitial.lastname@company.com`
- `firstname@company.com`
- `firstinitiallastname@company.com`

Send to all 5. Note which bounce. Done.

### Method 4: Third-party tools
- Apollo.io
- Hunter.io
- RocketReach
- Lusha

Mixed accuracy. Useful as backup.

## Pipeline management

A lightweight CRM is essential. At minimum:

| Field | Why |
|---|---|
| Name | Obvious |
| Role / Company | Context |
| Stakeholder category (1–7) | For tracking coverage |
| Sourcing method | To know what works |
| Status (Sourced / Reached / Followed / Replied / Scheduled / Done) | Pipeline |
| Email sent date | For follow-up timing |
| Notes | Conversation snippets, what they care about |
| Referred to / from | For the referral chain |
| Next action | What's next |

Tools:
- **Free / simple:** Notion table, Airtable, Google Sheets
- **Step up:** Streak (Gmail extension), Mixmax
- **More serious:** HubSpot CRM (free tier), Apollo

Don't over-engineer. The pipeline is for *thoughtfulness, not automation.*

## Follow-up discipline

**A short, well-timed follow-up doubles response rate.**

Day 5–7 after initial email, if no reply:

```
Hi [Name],

Just wanted to bump this in case it got buried.

If now isn't a good time, no worries — happy to circle back. Or if there's a 
better person on your team for this kind of conversation, that works too.

Best,
Alex
```

After one follow-up with no reply: **drop it.** Don't send 5 follow-ups. The recipient is not going to respond and you're damaging your reputation.

## Closing every conversation with intros

The single highest-leverage habit. Built into every interview:

*"Before we wrap — who else should I be talking to? Specifically [people in adjacent role, or different segment, or with strong opinions]."*

Then: *"Mind making an intro to [name]? Happy to draft an email you can forward, or you can connect us however's easiest."*

A "forward this" intro converts at 70–90%+. A name without an intro converts at 20–30%.

## Beyond email — non-traditional outreach

Three illustrative non-email approaches from past Stanford RDI teams:

- A team researching ports hired a **taco truck**, parked it by the docks, gave free tacos, and asked workers about their day. Wealth of information; high goodwill.
- A team **hosted a virtual conference** on their topic at the GSB. Gave insiders a platform, drew speakers eager for an academic audience, demonstrated the team's passion, and produced an attendee list that became further outreach fuel.
- A team **publicly published its learnings** as it went — articles, summaries, LinkedIn posts, industry magazine pieces. Originally intended as giving back, became their most powerful outreach channel: executives at the largest companies in their space reached out directly, leading to job offers, mentorship, investment.

Common thread: **reduce friction, demonstrate genuine interest, meet people where they already are.**

## Sharing back — the multiplier

The single most underleveraged activity:

**Directly with interviewees** — a follow-up that goes beyond "thank you." Reference something specific you learned and offer something in return: an article, a data point, a best practice you heard from someone in a similar role.

**Publicly** — share what you're learning. LinkedIn, Substack, industry pubs. Even one well-thought-out post per month establishes you as someone doing serious work in the space.

**The compounding effect:** people who'd never have replied to your cold email will reach out to you. Inbound replaces cold. The reputation you build through sharing becomes your most powerful sourcing tool.

## Common failure modes

| Failure | Fix |
|---|---|
| Sourcing only one stakeholder category | Audit weekly; you should have all 7 in the pipeline |
| All senior-execs in pipeline | Aim *lower* first; people closer to the work talk more |
| Boilerplate emails | Personalize; specifics |
| Pipeline stalls after 20 contacts | Push through the wall; momentum builds at 30–40 |
| Not asking for intros | Always close with the ask |
| Forgetting follow-ups | Calendar reminder at day 5 |
| Over-investing in tooling | A spreadsheet is fine; thoughtfulness > automation |
| Not sharing back | The lowest-effort, highest-leverage habit |
