# Stage 00 — The Prepared Mind

> Pasteur's principle: in observation, chance favors only the mind that is already prepared to notice it.

You are not yet an entrepreneur. You are an explorer.

This stage exists because most people skip it, and most people who skip it never recover. They commit to an idea before they have earned the right to recognize one — usually a brainstorm over a bottle of wine, a hackathon weekend, or a friend's pitch that sounded good. They spend the next two years pivoting in circles because the foundation underneath the original idea was never load-bearing.

The Prepared Mind stage is **the systematic construction of the depth of understanding that makes good ideas obvious to you and only you.** Its output is not a startup. Its output is a category of insight that only someone who has done the work could see.

## When you are in this stage

- You have a vague interest in a space (climate tech, healthcare ops, applied AI, a regulated industry) but no problem statement.
- You have an idea, but it came to you in five minutes and you cannot defend it.
- You have a hypothesis, but you cannot name three people who would actively look for what you'd build.
- You are between things and want your next bet to be one you can stay with for ten years.

If you have a desperate customer who is paying you money, you are not in this stage. Go to [Stage 06 — PMF measurement](06_pmf_measurement.md).

## What "good" looks like at the end of this stage

- You can be **cocktail-party fluent** in a space — vocabulary, players, dynamics, who's adjacent to whom, what's changing, what's stuck.
- You have a **research network** — 30–50+ people who'd take your call.
- You can articulate **what's changing in this space** and why now matters — internal change vectors (regulation, labor, consolidation) colliding with external ones (AI, demographics, capital costs).
- You have a **shortlist of problems worth pursuing**, each one passing through a deliberate filter (your opportunity rubric).
- You have **earned secrets** — beliefs about this space that you cannot Google and that surprised you when you formed them.
- You have **conviction** rather than enthusiasm.

## The methodology

The systematic discipline for this stage is **Research Driven Inspiration (RDI)** — see [`frameworks/rdi.md`](../frameworks/rdi.md) for the full treatment. The three-phase shape:

### Phase 1 — Team and alignment (1–2 weeks)

The work starts before the topic is locked in. You decide who you are doing this with, why, and how.

- **Self-reflect.** Working style, strengths, fears, what you will and won't tolerate. (See [`templates/self_reflection.md`](../templates/self_reflection.md).)
- **Pick research partners** if you want them (2–3 max). Diverse, complementary, compatible goals. Not co-founders. *Yet.* (If a research partner becomes a co-founder candidate later, the bar is higher — see the co-founder selection section in [`frameworks/rdi.md`](../frameworks/rdi.md).)
- **Build your opportunity rubric.** What does a good opportunity look like to you? Must-haves, deal-breakers, red flags. The act of writing this is more valuable than the rubric itself. (See [`templates/opportunity_rubric.md`](../templates/opportunity_rubric.md).)
- **Set norms.** Cadence, tools, how you'll make decisions when you disagree.

### Phase 2 — Bench analysis (2–4 weeks)

Get smart. Read everything. Map the space.

- **Pick a haystack** that's narrow enough to find experts in but broad enough to be surprised by. *"Indoor agriculture"* is a haystack; *"food"* is not.
- **Build an industry primer** — 5–10 pages: market dynamics, players, change vectors, vocabulary, open questions. (See [`templates/industry_primer.md`](../templates/industry_primer.md) and [`playbooks/build_industry_primer.md`](../playbooks/build_industry_primer.md).)
- **Map the ecosystem.** Who's up the value chain. Who's down. Who's adjacent. Who's regulator, who's customer, who's union, who's standards body.
- **Use AI as a research partner**, not a substitute. The test: can you defend the synthesis without referring back to what the AI told you? If no, you haven't learned it.

### Phase 3 — Primary research and synthesis (4–8 weeks, then cycles)

The engine. This phase does not have a single endpoint — it continues until you have conviction.

The cycle: **outreach → interview → synthesize → evaluate → refine**.

- **Reach out across all seven stakeholder categories** — incumbents (every level), startups, investors, experts, academics, regulators, customers. Aim *lower* in organizations than you think — the people doing the work explain it more honestly than the people running it. (See [`playbooks/run_outreach.md`](../playbooks/run_outreach.md).)
- **Run 50–100+ interviews.** Most people stop at 20 and miss the patterns that only show up after 40. The math compounds: the 50th interview is dramatically more valuable than the 5th because you bring more context to it.
- **Hold hypotheses lightly.** If you fall in love with one too early, you'll lead witnesses, hear what you want to hear, and waste the whole exercise. (See [`frameworks/the_mom_test.md`](../frameworks/the_mom_test.md).)
- **Synthesize continuously**, not at the end. Pile Building → Cluster Analysis → 2x2s → Stakeholder comparison → Hypothesis tracking. Each pass sharpens what you're hearing. (See [`playbooks/synthesis.md`](../playbooks/synthesis.md).)
- **Share what you're learning.** Both privately (back to the people you interviewed) and publicly (LinkedIn, Substack, industry pubs). This is the highest-leverage activity in the entire methodology — it generates inbound and converts you from "person doing research" to "person known for this space."

## The discipline

A few rules that distinguish RDI from "I'm doing research."

**1. Curiosity, not validation.** You are not testing an idea. You are forming one. If you find yourself trying to confirm what you already believe, you are not doing RDI anymore — you have skipped to Stage 02. (See [`frameworks/the_mom_test.md`](../frameworks/the_mom_test.md) for why this corrupts the data.)

**2. Earned secrets.** Peter Thiel's framing. The questions worth asking are the ones whose answers are: *"What do you believe is true that nearly nobody else does?"* If your interviews are not generating answers that surprise you, your questions are too generic or your interlocutors aren't the right ones.

**3. Look outside the building.** Inside the building (a class, a startup community, a Twitter timeline) you get the consensus. The earned secret lives in the conversations with retired regulators, frustrated engineers, supply-chain operations managers, the procurement department of an incumbent. Anywhere most founders aren't bothering to go.

**4. Reduce friction; demonstrate genuine interest.** Past RDI teams have met dock workers with a free taco truck, hosted virtual industry conferences as students, and published thoughtful pieces during research that turned into the most powerful recruiting tool they had. The common thread: be the most curious, generous, lowest-friction person to talk to in the space.

**5. Don't conflate "not yet" with "not ever."** Park ideas explicitly. Some interviews surface opportunities that aren't right for *this* team or *this* timing — they go in the parking lot, not the trash.

## Common traps in this stage

| Trap | What it looks like | Why it's a trap |
|---|---|---|
| Premature problem statement | "We're building X for Y" before Week 4 | You'll lead witnesses and end up validating a fantasy |
| Brainstorming as ideation | A whiteboard session before the research is done | Manufactures false-confidence ideas; you'll spend two years pivoting from one |
| Skipping the primer | Going straight to interviews | You'll burn warm intros on questions you could've Googled |
| Hiding | "I need to do more research before I talk to anyone" | The research IS the conversations. Reading more doesn't substitute. |
| Falling in love with one space too early | Locked in by week 3, blind to alternatives | The opportunity rubric exists to make this decision deliberate, not emotional |
| Treating every interview as a sales call | Pitching your idea instead of probing their reality | Either they get guarded or they get polite. Polite is worse — false-positive signal |
| Stopping at 20 interviews | "I'm hearing the same things" | You're hearing the same surface things. Depth shows up at 40–60. |

## Exit criteria — leaving this stage

You are ready to leave the Prepared Mind stage when you can answer **yes** to all of these:

1. Can I name 5+ specific people who would be considered experts in this space, and have I talked to most of them?
2. Can I articulate what is structurally changing in this space and why now?
3. Do I hold beliefs about this space that surprise informed people when I share them?
4. Have I identified **a specific problem** — not a space, a problem — that recurs unprompted across multiple stakeholder types?
5. Can I name the people who experience this problem most acutely, and how to find more of them?
6. Does this opportunity pass my own opportunity rubric on the must-haves?
7. Am I excited *and* honest about the failure modes?

If you can't answer yes, you are still in Stage 00. That's fine. Most teams stay here longer than they think they should — and the teams that did stay are the ones that ended up with conviction nobody else had.

When you can answer yes: proceed to [Stage 01 — Insight & idea selection](01_insight_and_idea.md).
