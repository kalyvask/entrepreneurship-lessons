# The Three Value Dimensions (Functional / Economic / Psychological)

> The lens of this framework: products don't really sell features. What they sell is a way for users to appear more professional, more capable, or more tasteful.

A framework for understanding *why* customers pay, and *how much*. Every benefit a product delivers falls into one of three dimensions. The dimension determines willingness-to-pay (WTP) and defensibility.

This is one of the highest-leverage lenses in product strategy because most founders — especially technical ones — over-index on the first dimension and ignore the third, which is where the money and the moat actually are.

## The three dimensions

### Functional value — "Does it work?"

Does the product solve the problem? Does it do the job?

- *"This tool reconciles our inventory in 1 hour instead of 15."*
- *"This API returns results in under 100ms."*

Functional value is **table stakes** — necessary but not sufficient. If the product doesn't work, nothing else matters. But functional value alone rarely commands premium pricing, because functionality is the easiest dimension for a competitor to copy. The moment a competitor matches your functionality, you're in a price war.

### Economic value — "Does it save me money or time?"

Quantifiable ROI. The rational purchase driver.

- *"This saves us 14 hours/week = $X in labor."*
- *"This reduces our error rate, avoiding $Y in rework."*

Economic value is what you put in the business case. It's how a buyer justifies the purchase to their boss. It supports rational, defensible pricing tied to the value delivered (value-based pricing). But it's also benchmarkable — a competitor can claim a better ROI, and you're back in a comparison battle.

### Psychological value — "Does it make me feel something?"

Status, identity, confidence, belonging, taste. The emotional and social dimension.

- *"Using this makes me look like a sophisticated operator to my CEO."*
- *"Carrying this brand signals I'm serious about my craft."*
- *"This gives me confidence I won't be blindsided in the board meeting."*

**Psychological value drives the highest willingness-to-pay and is the hardest to replicate.** A competitor can match your features and beat your ROI claim, but they can't easily copy what your product *means* to the customer's identity.

This is the dimension founders most often miss. The classic framing: *the "face product" — the one that makes the user look good — is often the most profitable.*

## Why the third dimension matters most

A product with only functional and economic value is **vulnerable to commoditization.** As soon as a competitor matches the function and the ROI, there's no reason to prefer you. You compete on price. Margins compress.

A product with strong psychological value has a moat that isn't on the spec sheet. The customer's preference is bound up with their identity, their status, their sense of competence. That's sticky in a way features never are.

**The diagnostic:** for your product, classify every benefit as F, E, or P. **If there is no P, you have a commoditization risk.** You need to either find the psychological dimension (who does this product make the user, in their own eyes and their peers' eyes?) or accept that you'll compete on price.

## Different segments, different value

A critical subtlety: **different customer segments derive different sources of value from the same product.**

- For one segment, your product is primarily functional (they just need the job done).
- For another, it's economic (they're cost-driven, ROI-focused).
- For another, it's psychological (they care how it makes them look).

The same product, three value propositions, three messages, three price points. The mistake is using one message for all three.

This connects directly to the *who* in your value hypothesis (see [`frameworks/pmf.md`](pmf.md)) and to JTBD's emotional/social dimensions (see [`frameworks/jobs_to_be_done.md`](jobs_to_be_done.md)). Narrowing the *who* often means narrowing to the segment for whom your product has the strongest psychological value — because that's the segment with the highest WTP and the most desperation.

## Painkillers vs. vitamins

A related distinction, sometimes called the must-have test:

- **Painkillers** solve a problem the customer *has to* solve. They address a severe, present pain. The customer is actively looking for a solution and will pay to make the pain stop.
- **Vitamins** are nice-to-have. They improve things marginally. The customer would be fine without them.

**Pain intensity is pricing power.** A painkiller for a severe, frequent, expensive pain commands premium pricing and short sales cycles. A vitamin requires you to convince the customer they should care — a much harder, slower, lower-margin sale.

The connection to desperation: a desperate customer (see [`frameworks/pmf.md`](pmf.md)) is, by definition, looking for a painkiller. If your product is a vitamin to everyone you talk to, you don't have a desperate customer, and you don't have PMF coming.

## Applying it

For your current product, run this analysis:

1. **List every benefit your product delivers.**
2. **Classify each: F, E, or P.**
3. **Is there a P?** If not — what could it be? Who does this product make the user, socially and emotionally? If you genuinely can't find a P, you're at commoditization risk — plan to compete on price or rethink.
4. **Painkiller or vitamin?** For your target *who* — is this solving a severe present pain, or improving something marginally? If vitamin — that's a desperation problem; revisit the *who*.
5. **Segment the value.** Do different segments value this differently? If so, which segment derives the most psychological value? That's likely your sharpest *who*.

## Common failure modes

| Failure | Why it kills you |
|---|---|
| Selling only functional value | Commoditizes the moment a competitor matches features |
| No psychological dimension identified | Vulnerable to price competition; no identity moat |
| One message for all segments | You dilute; each segment hears value that isn't theirs |
| Building a vitamin and treating it like a painkiller | Long sales cycles, low WTP, no desperation, no PMF |
| Engineers pricing on cost-plus | Ignores the value-based and psychological premium you could capture |
| Assuming your value is the customer's value | You think it's functional; they buy it for status — and you message it wrong |
