# Disruption Theory (Clayton Christensen)

> *"Companies fail BECAUSE they do everything right — listen to best customers, invest in highest-margin products, pursue large markets. This blinds them to disruptive threats."* — paraphrased from Clayton Christensen, *The Innovator's Dilemma*

The most commonly misused concept in entrepreneurship. "Disruptive" gets applied to any new product. The technical definition is narrower and more useful: a *disruptive innovation* is one that starts by serving an **underserved or unserved** market segment with a **simpler, cheaper, more convenient** alternative — and then improves over time to capture the mainstream.

Most innovations are **sustaining**, not disruptive. Telling the two apart matters because they require completely different strategies, and a startup pursuing a *sustaining* path against incumbents almost always loses.

## Sustaining vs. Disruptive

**Sustaining innovation** — better products for existing customers on the existing performance trajectory. The customer cares about more of what they already get: faster, cheaper, more features, higher reliability.

- iPhone 14 → iPhone 15.
- Excel 2003 → Excel 365.
- Salesforce v1 → Salesforce v100.

**Who wins sustaining battles:** incumbents. Almost always. They have customer relationships, channels, brand, capital, and incremental improvement processes optimized for exactly this kind of work. A startup that tries to make a "better Salesforce" or "better Slack" almost always loses, because the incumbents have asymmetric advantages on sustaining axes.

**Disruptive innovation** — products that are *worse on the dimensions incumbents' customers value*, but *better on dimensions those customers don't yet value* (or simpler, cheaper, more convenient). They target either:

- **Nonconsumers** — people not buying anything because the existing options are too expensive, complicated, or inaccessible.
- **Overserved customers** — people who would gladly take "good enough" at a lower price/complexity if it existed.

**Who wins disruptive battles:** entrants. Almost always. Because the incumbent's organization is structurally incapable of pursuing a disruptive innovation profitably (more on this below).

## The two flavors of disruption

| Type | Mechanism | Who it targets | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Low-end disruption** | Overserved customers buying a good-enough, cheaper alternative | Existing market customers at the low end | Discount retail, minimills, Honda motorcycles (low-end of the US market), generic drugs |
| **New-market disruption** | Nonconsumers — people who weren't buying anything | New customer segments outside the existing market | Sony transistor radio (kids who couldn't afford tabletop radios), iPod (people who weren't buying portable music), Airbnb (people who weren't booking hotels for that trip) |

**Low-end:** the incumbent's customers were paying for performance they didn't value. A simpler, cheaper alternative captures them. Incumbents resist because the low end is the worst margin segment — they're often happy to "trade up" and let the entrant have it.

**New-market:** the incumbent's product was too expensive or complicated for a whole population. A simpler, cheaper alternative makes consumption possible. Incumbents don't notice because the entrant isn't competing for their customers — until the entrant moves upmarket.

## The litmus test

Christensen's four questions to identify a disruptive opportunity:

1. **Are there nonconsumers or overserved customers?** Is there a population not buying because existing options are too expensive/complicated? Or paying for features they don't use?
2. **Is your offering simpler, cheaper, or more convenient on a new dimension?** Not "better on the same axis" — different axis.
3. **Do incumbents have asymmetric motivation to ignore this?** Would pursuing this opportunity destroy more of their existing business than it creates? Would it require them to operate at margins that violate their cost structure?
4. **Is there a viable upmarket path?** Can you start at the low end / new market and improve over time to eventually compete in the mainstream?

If **all four** are yes, you have a disruptive opportunity. If you can only say yes to one or two, you may have a sustaining product — which means you're competing with incumbents on their terms.

## Why great companies fail (the Innovator's Dilemma)

The provocative finding of Christensen's research: **well-managed companies fail not by mismanagement but by good management** — the same disciplines that build dominant incumbents also blind them to disruption.

Five root causes:

### 1. Resource Dependence

Customers control the company's resource allocation through their purchasing decisions. When customers reward sustaining innovations (more features, faster, more reliable), the company's R&D and revenue pipelines reinforce sustaining work. Disruptive innovations are rejected by the customers — *"why are you investing in this cheap, simple, inferior thing?"* — and so the company stops investing in them.

A startup is not constrained this way. *You don't yet have customers whose preferences shape your portfolio.* You can chase disruption.

### 2. RPV Mismatch (Resources, Processes, Values)

Christensen's framework for understanding what an organization can and cannot do:

- **Resources** = what the organization *has* — capital, people, technology, brand, IP.
- **Processes** = how the organization *does work* — the patterns and methods for delivering on its strategy.
- **Values** = what the organization *prioritizes* — margin thresholds, market sizes, cost structures it can support.

An organization's RPV is optimized for the work it's done before. It is **structurally incapable** of pursuing work that requires a different RPV. A company built to sell $100k-a-year enterprise contracts cannot pursue a $99/year SaaS product profitably — their RPV's *values* (gross margin requirements, sales cycle assumptions, customer success cost structure) reject it.

Implication for a startup: **identify what incumbents *can't* do without rewriting their RPV.** That's where you have structural advantage. (Related: Rachleff on insight → "structural reason incumbents are blind to it" — see [`frameworks/rachleff_pmf.md`](rachleff_pmf.md).)

### 3. Upmarket Migration

Companies naturally migrate upmarket over time. Higher margins, larger contracts, more sophisticated customers. The pull is hard to resist — investors love it, sales reps prefer it, executives are rewarded for it.

The cost: companies vacate the low end. Their cost structures rise. Their organizational appetite for small deals disappears. Markets that used to be central become "too small."

A disruptive entrant fills the vacated space. They climb upmarket later. By the time the incumbent notices, the entrant has scale, references, and cost-structure advantages.

### 4. Performance Oversupply

Technology often improves faster than customer demand. A product can become *more capable than its customers need.* When that happens, the **basis of competition shifts.** Customers stop paying premiums for additional performance; they start prioritizing other dimensions — price, convenience, simplicity, integration.

The basis-of-competition shift opens the door for disruptors offering "good enough" + cheaper/simpler.

The **basis-of-competition sequence** (Christensen): functionality → reliability → convenience → price. As one dimension becomes "good enough," the next dimension becomes the basis of competition. Disruption windows open at every shift.

### 5. Bad Money

Pressure for fast, large-scale revenue forces sustaining strategies and kills disruptive experiments. When investors push for top-line growth, the easiest path is to upsell existing customers — *not* to develop a new disruptive product line that may take years to grow.

Implication: many disruptive innovations require *patient capital* in the early years. Investors who optimize for quick wins push their portfolio companies into sustaining mode by accident.

## How startups actually disrupt — practical pattern

The Christensen-aligned playbook for a startup:

1. **Pick a market where performance has overshot demand.** Look for customer segments paying for features they don't use, or paying premiums for performance they don't value.
2. **Target either nonconsumers or the overserved.** Not the mainstream. The mainstream has incumbents who'll crush a sustaining attack.
3. **Design a product that's worse on traditional axes but better on new ones** (simpler, cheaper, more convenient, accessible).
4. **Operate at margins and cost structures incumbents cannot match without restructuring.**
5. **Win the niche.** Build credibility, references, scale.
6. **Improve upmarket steadily.** Add features and capabilities over time, climbing toward the incumbent's territory.
7. **By the time the incumbent recognizes the threat, the cost of responding exceeds what they can justify.**

A famous version: **Salesforce.** Cloud CRM in 2000 was *worse* than Siebel on traditional axes (limited functionality, no customization, "just" subscription). Better on new axes (no IT, fast to deploy, no upfront cost). Siebel's RPV — enterprise sales motion, perpetual licenses, on-prem deployment — couldn't profitably pursue cloud SaaS at the price points Salesforce offered. By the time Siebel tried, Salesforce had the references, the cost structure, and the developer ecosystem locked in.

(Rachleff uses this case explicitly in his framework's *Defining the How* segment.)

## What disruption is NOT

- "Tech-enabled" doesn't mean disruptive. (Most "tech-enabled" services compete on sustaining axes.)
- "AI-powered" doesn't mean disruptive. (Adding AI to an existing product is usually sustaining, not disruptive.)
- "Lower priced" alone doesn't mean disruptive. (Without simpler/more convenient/different basis of competition, it's just commodity competition.)
- "Better product" certainly doesn't mean disruptive. (That's classic sustaining innovation.)
- "Marketplace" doesn't mean disruptive.

Most "disruption" in pitch decks is marketing language. Genuine disruption — in Christensen's technical sense — is rarer than most founders claim.

## A practical exercise

For your current value hypothesis, answer:

1. Are you competing on the same axes as the incumbents (sustaining) or different axes (disruptive)?
2. If sustaining: what's your structural advantage that lets you out-execute the incumbent? (Be honest. If the answer is "we're scrappier and faster," that's not durable.)
3. If disruptive: who are the nonconsumers or overserved? What's their current alternative? Why hasn't the incumbent served them?
4. What is the incumbent's *asymmetric motivation* — what would they have to give up to chase you? (Their margins? Their channels? Their existing customers? Their organizational identity?)
5. What's your upmarket path? Five years from now, how have you climbed?

If you can't answer these clearly, your "disruption" claim probably isn't disruption. That's fine — but be honest about what game you're playing.
