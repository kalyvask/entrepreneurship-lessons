# Research Driven Inspiration (RDI)

> The framework takes its name from Louis Pasteur's observation that, in research, chance favors the prepared mind — the person who has done the groundwork to recognize an opportunity when it appears.

RDI is a structured framework for building what its authors call a **prepared mind**: a depth of understanding of an industry or space that lets you see where it's heading and act on it before others can. Instead of starting with a solution and trying to validate it, you start with curiosity about a space and let discovery drive you toward conviction — and ultimately toward a hypothesis about the future: *what's changing, what's becoming possible, and where the opportunity lives.*

Developed by Scott Brady and Brett Jordan.

This file is a condensed reference for RDI.

## The intellectual heritage

RDI draws from:

- **Pasteur's *prepared mind*** — observation favors those who have done the prep work.
- **Peter Thiel and Mike Maples' *earned secrets*** — insights you can't Google, only discover through deliberate inquiry, real conversations, and sharp synthesis.
- **Jim Ellis's *high-tide markets*** — industries that are growing and accumulating value.
- **Porter's Five Forces / PIE framework** — for analyzing competitive dynamics.

The claim: by deliberately synthesizing across the experiences of thought leaders, stakeholders, and industry experts, an outsider can spot gaps, shifts, and second-order effects that even insiders miss. Where insiders are siloed, you are unconstrained.

## When to use RDI

RDI sits **upstream of every other decision** an entrepreneur makes. It is the journey's beginning, not its end. It is a **prerequisite** for ideation, customer development, MVP scoping, fundraising, hiring, or any commitment of real resources.

Contrast with Lean Startup / Customer Development: those methodologies start with a falsifiable business-model hypothesis (e.g., "we believe customer X has problem Y, and product Z will solve it"). RDI starts earlier: with the recognition that you do not yet know which problem matters, and that the act of figuring that out is itself a structured discipline.

RDI's output is the *input* to Lean Startup or Customer Development. They are sequenced, not competing.

## The three things that must be present at once

RDI works because mindset, strategy, and process reinforce each other.

- **Mindset** — exploration first, not validation. You're not starting with a problem and working toward a solution. You're starting with curiosity. Requires humility and willingness to engage with strangers.
- **Strategy** — learning efficiently by standing on the shoulders of the people in a space. Industry insiders typically can't do this; they don't talk to competitors, regulators, technologists, or adjacent customers. As an outsider, you can.
- **Process** — turning the above into momentum through 100+ conversations across roles, companies, and vantage points. The most successful teams have well over a hundred.

Remove any one and the system breaks down.

## The three phases

### Phase 1: Team formation and alignment

Counterintuitive: the team forms **before the problem is selected.** Why? Because RDI partners are not co-founders. They are research partners bound by a common interest in *doing the research*.

Sub-steps:
1. **Self-reflection** — motivations, working style, must-haves, deal-breakers
2. **Building your team** — typically 2–3; diverse and complementary > all-the-same
3. **Going solo** — possible but harder; build replacement support systems deliberately
4. **Internal alignment** — goals must be *compatible*, values *shared*
5. **Setting norms** — operating cadence, communication, decision-making
6. **Defining success → opportunity rubric** — must-haves, nice-to-haves, deal-breakers, red flags
7. **Extended support systems** — advisors, river guides, peer thought partners
8. **Getting organized** — workspace, tools, AI as research companion

#### Co-founder selection (deeper treatment of step 2–4)

If you're picking a co-founder rather than just RDI research partners, the bar is higher. A co-founder is the person you'll fight with, hire with, fire with, and run a board with for the next decade. The wrong choice is one of the single most expensive mistakes a founder can make.

**The right co-founder has** complementary skills (not just additive), enough overlapping mental model that you can "mind-meld" on big decisions, and enough independence that you can disagree, argue, and repair without it ending the relationship.

**Don't pick cold.** Picking a co-founder from a meetup, a LinkedIn intro, or a "co-founder dating" event is the *exception* path. It can work, but the failure rate is high. Default path: start from your existing network — people you've worked with, classmates, colleagues. Lean on super-connectors (investors, mentors, former bosses) to surface candidates.

**The best filter is working on something together.** A few months of side-by-side work on a hard project — not coffee, not resumes — tells you what you need to know:
- Do we make each other better?
- Can we disagree without it becoming personal?
- Do our work styles compose (one's strengths covering the other's gaps)?
- Do we both want to keep going at the end?

Treat the trial period explicitly. Set check-ins. Be honest about what's not working before it compounds.

**Set expectations early and revisit them often.** Sit down explicitly (in writing) on:
- Roles and responsibilities (who owns what, who decides what)
- Equity split (and vesting — usually 4 years, 1-year cliff)
- Board structure (who's on it, how votes work)
- Desired outcomes (what does success look like — venture-scale exit? lifestyle? IPO?)
- Personal concerns and fears (what would break this for you?)
- What happens if one of us leaves
- How we'll handle disagreement

This is not a one-time conversation. Revisit as the company evolves. The thing you agreed on at incorporation may need updating at Series A.

**Solo founders.** Not every great company has two names on the masthead. Solo founders succeed, but the bar is the same as for co-founded teams from an investor's perspective. You need **bedrock conviction** in yourself: deep domain knowledge, a special skill, or the ability to recruit and inspire the early team.

Four paths if you don't have a co-founder yet:
- **Start solo and stay solo** — if your skill profile covers the bases and you have a strong network for hiring
- **Start solo, add a co-founder later** — risky on equity / control negotiation; do this only if you have a clear plan
- **Wait until you find the right person** — slows you down but reduces the largest single risk
- **Decide not to do it** — sometimes the honest answer

Better to be **solo and strong than partnered and misaligned.** A misaligned co-founder is worse than no co-founder.

**Outputs:** team formed (or explicit solo decision), opportunity rubric, goals and norms documented, written agreement on roles / equity / outcomes.

### Phase 2: Ramp-up and bench-level analysis

Get smart. Secondary research to become "**cocktail-party conversational**" in the space.

1. **Picking a haystack** — narrow enough to find experts, broad enough to be surprised
   - Two filters: team fit (curiosity, background relevance) + market fit (high-tide, change vectors, fragmentation)
2. **Dialing in scope** — survey broadly first ("bare eyes, then binoculars"), then narrow
   - Too broad: can't find experts ("food"). Too narrow: you've already decided.
3. **Building a primer** — 5–10 pages, vocabulary, players, dynamics, change forces, open questions. Not a deliverable — a process.
4. **AI as research partner** — accelerator, not substitute. The test: can you defend the synthesis without referring back to what the AI generated?

**Outputs:** focus area + market hypothesis, industry primer, ecosystem map, initial outreach list, preliminary questions.

### Phase 3: Research, synthesis, and evaluation

The engine. A **cyclical process** that runs until conviction.

The cycle: **outreach → interview → synthesize → evaluate → refine.**

Sub-steps:
1. Who to learn from (seven stakeholder categories)
2. Running your outreach
3. Questions that unlock real learning
4. Running effective interviews
5. Connecting the dots (synthesis)
6. Sharing what you're learning
7. Refine, refocus, repeat
8. Diving deeper (focused 2-week sprints on emerging opportunities)

**Outputs:** deep expertise, a clear view of the most important problems, tested hypotheses with conviction, a network of relationships, foundation for next steps.

## Who to learn from

Seven stakeholder categories — explicitly target across all of them:

1. **Established players** — incumbents at every level (CEOs, VPs, Directors, Managers, ICs). Strategy looks different from execution.
2. **Startups** — founders and early team members. (Caveat: startups are in problem-solving mode. Problems worth identifying are often ones no founder has yet seen.)
3. **Investors** — partners, principals, focus-area leads. Especially **corporate venture arms** — intersection of incumbents and startups.
4. **Experts and thought leaders** — authors, journalists, retired insiders, consultants. **River guides.**
5. **Academics** — institutions with named centers, top citation authors. Tech transfer offices for spinout signals.
6. **Government, policy, regulatory** — agency staff, program leads, former regulators.
7. **Customers and users** — direct and adjacent.

**Where to start:** aim *lower* than the C-suite first. Operators are more willing to educate, more tolerant of foundational questions, more excited to share. *By the time you reach the CEO, you may know things about their company that they don't.*

## Outreach mechanics

**Two approaches to email:**
- **Semi-tailored batched** — for common roles across many companies. ~20–30% response rate.
- **Fully personalized** — for irreplaceable people. Can exceed 90% response rate.

**Anatomy of an outreach email:**
- **Sender address:** `.edu` or institutional address is a huge unlock
- **Subject line:** answers *who are you* and *what do you want*
- **Body — five elements:**
    1. Introduce yourself and your connection to the space
    2. Explain why them — what about their experience is uniquely valuable
    3. Find connections — mutual contacts, articles they've written, events
    4. Offer something — to share your learnings
    5. Clear ask and next steps

**Before sending:** check spelling of every name. Read aloud. Plan the follow-up — short, well-timed follow-up can double response rate.

**Sourcing emails:**
- Best source: a referral with their email format
- Email syntax inference (try first.last, first_last, etc.)
- Conference attendee lists (look for `filetype:pdf` or `filetype:xlsx`)
- Third-party contact tools

**Why people respond to RDI researchers:** you're not asking for something, you're offering something — curiosity, fresh perspective, willingness to share back. The moment outreach feels like networking or selling, you lose what makes it work.

See [`playbooks/run_outreach.md`](../playbooks/run_outreach.md) for full operational detail.

## Interview philosophy

The purpose of RDI interviews is **not** to collect opinions, validate hypotheses, or extract specific data points. It is to **understand someone's lived experience** within an industry — their concrete stories, the friction they actually encounter.

RDI interviews are **forward-from-curiosity, not backward-from-an-answer.**

The discipline:
- **Hold hypotheses lightly.** Wanting a hypothesis to be true is natural; trying to make it true corrupts learning. Be just as excited about disproving as proving.
- **Avoid sales-call drift.** When research conversations turn into pitches, people get guarded or polite. Polite is worse — you walk away feeling validated when the signal isn't real.
- **Open-ended questions by default.** Start with *what, why, how*. Avoid *can, do, are* (closed yes/no).
- **Probing follow-ups are where the gold lives.** Most questions you ask should be real-time responses to what you're hearing.
- **Pressure-test problems.** People are eager to be helpful; they offer problems they think you want. Probe: *Why does that matter? What's the impact? Have you tried to solve it?*
- **Broad to narrow flow.** Start high level; let themes emerge; narrow later.

Question categories (use as lenses, not a script):
- Their company and role
- Issues and obstacles ("there's gotta be a better way")
- Risks, fears, future failures (premortem framing)
- Unlocks and enabling factors (magic-wand questions)
- Shifts and disruptions (what's different now)
- Best practices (who's tackled this well)

**Don't over-script.** Interview guides are guides, not scripts.

## Running interviews

Pre-interview: 10–20 min of background research. Logistics ready. Consent for recording (legally required in many U.S. states).

In conversation:
- 30 minutes is plenty
- **90/10 rule** — listen 90%, speak 10%
- Don't fear silence — pauses precede the most thoughtful responses
- Listen for: emotions, frames, inconsistencies, surprises
- Close by asking for introductions and offering to share back

Post-interview wrap-up (20–30 min, immediately):
- Capture surprises, emotion, stories
- Clean up notes
- Pull exact quotes
- Distinguish facts from interpretation
- Update tracker, log follow-ups, send specific thank-you

## Synthesis methods

Synthesis is the bridge between exploration and clarity.

- **Pile Building** — convert each source (interview, article, report) into discrete, tagged scraps you can work with
- **Cluster Analysis** — group scraps into distinct, active claims. Name clusters with insight-oriented labels ("Data exists, but no one trusts it" — not "Data Issues")
- **Mapping and Process Diagrams** — value chains, workflows, regulatory flows. Tensions in the system become visible.
- **Attribute Maps / 2×2 Frameworks** — plot players/products along two dimensions. The first axes are rarely the best — the insight comes on iteration 2 or 3.
- **Stakeholder Comparison** — lay perspectives side by side. Disconnects between executives and operators are signal, not noise.
- **Hypothesis Tracking** — running list of explicit hypotheses with supporting evidence, contradicting evidence, confidence levels.

**Tensions between clusters are signal, not noise.** Contradictions point to where the next round of research should go.

## Sharing — direct and public

One of the highest-leverage activities in the framework. Sharing transforms one-time conversations into ongoing relationships and generates inbound momentum.

**Directly with interviewees:** specific follow-up (reference something they said) + offer something back (a relevant article, a pattern you've seen elsewhere).

**Publicly:**
- Start by putting it in the world that you're exploring this space and why
- Observations as they develop
- Structured perspectives (synthesis, framework, take)
- Substantive pieces (white paper, longform article)

Past RDI teams have substantially reduced cold outreach once inbound momentum built.

## Common pitfalls

- Treating idea generation as an event rather than a process
- Starting with a solution and searching for validation
- Scoping too broadly (can't find experts) or too narrowly (you've already decided)
- Tunnel vision from deep technical expertise
- Leading questions and confirmation bias
- Research conversations drifting into sales calls
- Over-scripting interviews
- Skipping recording / relying on paraphrased notes
- Treating problems people offer at face value
- AI as crutch instead of partner
- Forcing clarity too early in synthesis
- Endless data-collection hoping it resolves itself
- Inconsistent rubric application
- Holding too many options too long
- Treating sharing as a late-stage activity
