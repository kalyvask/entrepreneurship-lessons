# Career Strategy — A Separate Layer

> **This is off-spine.** The rest of this repo is about building a company from a curious mind to
> product-market fit. This folder is a different question: **what job to take and how to manage a
> career** — especially for someone leaving a top MBA program who wants to end up building or joining
> startups. It is kept separate on purpose; it is not part of the 00→07 journey, the venture
> workspace, or the rubrics. Use it when the question is "where should I work," not "how do I build
> this."

The through-line: **careers compound, and they are path-dependent.** The first few roles shape how the
market sees you, who you meet, and what options you get later. Early prestige (title, big offer, hot
label) is often a trap; what compounds is working with excellent people inside a company that is
winning, in a field you care about, while becoming unusually good at one thing.

The useful filter for any choice: *does this make me more successful, more skilled, and more credible
in three years — or does it just make me feel important today?*

---

## 1. Optimize first for being associated with success

The highest-priority objective leaving school is not money, title, or even learning in the abstract —
it is to become associated with a company that wins. The market gives you more credit than you deserve
for time at a successful company and less than you deserve for time at an unsuccessful one. Success is
simultaneously a credential, a school, and a network: winning companies attract strong people, teach
what "good" looks like, and surface future co-founders.

## 2. Do not join a startup before product-market fit (unless you're a founder or one of the first few)

Joining pre-PMF as employee #20 is taking founder-level risk without founder-level upside. The deeper
cost is reputational: most startups fail, and a failed company on your record is often discounted even
if your own work was excellent. One weak company choice is forgivable; two consecutive failures and
the market starts to judge you harshly, pushing you toward yet another low-quality employer willing to
hand you a title. (This is the *career* reason to respect PMF — distinct from the *building* reason in
`frameworks/pmf.md`.)

## 3. Target a mid-sized company with momentum, not a giant

"Successful" does not mean "large." A large tech company often operates from a near-monopoly position,
and most of the skills you build there — leveraging an installed base, brand, distribution, and
infrastructure — do not transfer to a startup. If you intend to build or join something small later,
learn in an environment that resembles it. The target profile:

| Criterion | Aim for |
|---|---|
| Revenue | ~$20M–$300M |
| Growth | ~50%+ expected for the next three years |
| Market | Large enough for a long run |
| Business model | Economically sound |
| Stage | Post-PMF, not too early |

At that stage you can work on a real new-product rollout or extension — lessons that *do* transfer.

## 4. Industry first, company second, position a distant third

Choose the field you want to be in for the long run, then the best company in it, and only then the
role. Most people invert this and optimize for title and scope — and two years on they are usually in a
less important position than classmates who took an unglamorous role at an excellent, fast-growing
company. The company most willing to hand you a big title early is often the one that cannot attract
better people. Take the modest role at the excellent company over the impressive role at the weak one;
a strong company promotes from within because there are never enough good people. Check the ego at the
door.

## 5. Don't optimize first-job compensation

Treat a career like a business plan: you don't maximize year-one profit, you maximize long-term value.
The real money is made later, as an early employee or executive at a company that wins — so the first
job's job is to set up jobs two and three. The best companies often pay *less* because people want to
be there; chasing the highest offer signals you can't tell the difference in company quality.

## 6. Don't be an offer collector

Do the work up front: decide which companies you actually want before interviewing in earnest. Practice
on lower-priority companies, then go after the top of your list once your story is sharp. Shopping
offers reads, to a strong hiring manager, as a lack of conviction. Aim the rifle; don't fire the
shotgun.

## 7. Don't panic about AI FOMO

Build scarce skills and a track record, not a label. Early in any hot technology cycle, the consensus
ideas are the obvious ones — and obvious rarely wins big. The most valuable companies in a wave tend
to arrive a few years in, around non-obvious uses. Rushing into a mediocre company because it carries
the current label is the mistake; position yourself to be attractive to the *better* opportunities
when they appear. (The "right and non-consensus" logic is the same as `frameworks/pmf.md` and
`stages/01_insight_and_idea.md` — here applied to a career rather than a company.)

## 8. Be great at one indispensable thing

Being broadly good at many things may get you into a top program; it does not make you indispensable
afterward. Companies don't urgently need more generalists — they need someone clearly exceptional at
one high-leverage thing (product judgment, enterprise sales, technical depth, finance, distribution,
recruiting). Stop rounding out weaknesses; find the strength that is authentic to you and make it
excellent.

## 9. Judgment is the most indispensable skill of all

The scarce skill underneath the rest is judgment — deciding well under uncertainty. The full treatment
is on-spine in [`frameworks/judgment_and_pareto.md`](../frameworks/judgment_and_pareto.md); it belongs
to building and to careers equally. Optimize for the magnitude of your right calls, not the frequency.

## 10. Mentors can be observed, not just befriended

You don't need a formal relationship to learn from someone excellent. Pick a person on a fast track
whose skill is authentic to you, and study how they think, prioritize, and decide — especially what
they choose to ignore. Pattern recognition from observation often beats a warm but low-signal
relationship.

## 11. Most advice is autobiographical — inspect the logic

People mostly advise you to do what they did. That doesn't make it wrong, but it means you can't
outsource the decision: understand the reasoning, context, and incentives behind each recommendation,
find where the logic breaks for your situation, and integrate. There is no single correct answer.

## 12. Sales and finance are underrated paths

Two functions top-MBAs routinely skip can be exceptional routes to leadership. An analytically strong
person in sales who cares about *profit*, not just quota, is rare and CEO-shaped. Premier-program CFOs
are scarce and tend to do very well. Don't dismiss the unglamorous functions with real operating
leverage.

## 13. Be cautious with chief-of-staff (and 2-year VC associate) roles

Proximity to a CEO is not the same as an operating skill. The risk of a chief-of-staff role — or a
fixed-term VC associate program — is two years that build no concrete, transferable capability and can
inflate your self-perception about the level you belong at, leaving you unqualified for a functional
leadership role afterward. Optimize for the rate at which you *learn a usable skill*. The exception:
if it is genuinely the only way into an excellent, fast-growing company, take it — with a clear plan
to move into an entry-level functional role and actually build the skill.

## 14. If you're unhappy, leave before it damages your work

Don't grind to a one-year mark for resume optics. Unhappiness degrades your work; degraded work can get
you fired; and being fired reads worse than a short stint. One short tenure is forgivable; repeated
short stints are not. Apply the lessons here to land somewhere good, then correct quickly if you
mislanded — you have decades of work ahead, and one job won't define the arc.

## Relationship to the rest of the repo

These ideas rhyme with the building spine but serve a different decision:

- **PMF** shows up here as a *career* signal (don't join pre-PMF), not as something you're building.
- **Non-consensus / right** shows up as how to read the job market, not the product market.
- **Judgment + Pareto** is shared infrastructure — see the on-spine framework.

Keep them separate in practice. A founder mid-journey should be in the stages and the workspace, not
here. This layer is for the "where do I work" question, which most users of this repo will face before
or after a venture, not during one.
