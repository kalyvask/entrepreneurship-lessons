# Crossing the Chasm (Geoffrey Moore)

Required reading for any tech startup. Rachleff calls it *"one of the great books."*

The book's thesis: there is a **structural break** in the technology adoption lifecycle — between *early adopters* and the *early majority* — that ends most startup companies. Companies that have early-adopter customers and assume those customers' enthusiasm will propagate to the broader market are wrong. The market behaves differently at the next stage. **The chasm is a whole-product problem, not a marketing problem.**

## The five psychographics

Crucial word: **psychographic.** Describes how someone thinks about buying, not who they are. A small startup and a Fortune 500 can both behave like early adopters if the buyer's mindset is the same.

### Innovators (technology enthusiasts)

Buy for joy. Buy because it's new. Don't influence early adopters. Worthless strategically.

In 2026 AI: the people running AI sandboxes for fun, generating images, testing every new model. *"They don't buy more of anything. They don't influence anyone else."*

You will get traction with innovators easily. You will not get a business from them.

### Early adopters (visionaries / Blank's earlyvangelists)

**This is the only group you should target for value-hypothesis validation.**

- Have a **burning problem** (often a strategic objective tied to their career or organization)
- Want **breakthroughs**, not improvements
- **Buy on proof of concept** — see it work once, sign the contract
- **Tolerate broken products** because the upside justifies the friction
- Their motivation: *"If I can solve this problem with this new tool, I'll get promoted."*

The early adopter is a visionary inside an organization that's risk-tolerant *just enough* to bet on you. They are bridge builders — they put their reputation on a possibility nobody else sees.

### Early majority (pragmatists)

The largest segment. Where the actual revenue lives.

- Want **productivity improvement**, not revolution
- **Buy only on references** from peers — never on vision
- **Demand the whole product** — every integration, every service, every guarantee
- Loyal once won. Hate switching costs.

They are not interested in your story. They are interested in *whether their peers use it and whether it's complete enough to deploy without surprises.*

### Late majority (conservatives)

Buy only after the product is the standard. Risk-averse. Price-sensitive. Demand turnkey solutions.

### Laggards

Never buy.

## The chasm

Between *early adopters* (visionaries) and *early majority* (pragmatists) is a structural gap. Specifically:

- Visionaries **buy on vision.** Pragmatists **buy on proof.**
- Visionaries **tolerate broken products.** Pragmatists **demand complete solutions.**
- Visionaries **don't need references.** Pragmatists **won't buy without them.**

The Catch-22: pragmatists won't buy without references; you can't get references from pragmatists without pragmatist customers.

**The chasm is not a marketing problem. It is a whole-product problem.**

Companies that have early adopters and assume the market will widen naturally are surprised when growth flatlines. They've sold to everyone who buys on vision; nobody else is going to. The next sale requires fundamentally different selling, fundamentally different product, and the company often doesn't recognize the transition is happening until it's too late.

## The whole product

The complete solution a pragmatist needs to succeed. Concentric rings around the MVP:

```
                    Ecosystem partners
                  Consultants, resellers
               Industry-specific customization
            Documentation, training, reliability
          System interfaces, integrations
        Supporting services
      Core (generic) product — your MVP
```

For early adopters, **the whole product IS the MVP.** They will build the rest of the ring themselves — their internal IT will integrate, their team will train themselves, they'll write their own documentation.

For pragmatists, the **whole product is non-negotiable.** They will not buy a product that requires them to do significant work to get value.

**Building the whole product can take years.** The MVP and the whole product are *opposites — two ends of a spectrum.* Stay in the MVP as long as you can. Building whole-product capabilities before the market validates the core value is one of the most expensive failure modes — you've consumed cash building support for a market that turned out not to be desperate.

## The bowling pin model

Moore's framework for crossing the chasm.

Pre-chasm: you've sold to early adopters across various segments. The temptation: try to sell to *everyone in the early majority* at once. Spread your effort thin. Build features for all segments simultaneously. Generic positioning.

This fails. Because pragmatists buy on references from *peers* — meaning *people who look like them, in their industry, with their constraints.* A general-purpose product with general-purpose positioning has no peer references for any specific pragmatist.

The solution: **bowling pins.**

Pick **one specific niche** of pragmatists. Become *the* product for them. Build the whole product specifically for that niche. Build references — testimonials, case studies, ROI numbers — that pragmatists in that niche find credible.

When you've owned that niche (the "head pin"), the *adjacent niches* become reachable. Because pragmatists in the adjacent niche know pragmatists in the head pin. They start to ask. References flow naturally.

Bowling pin 1 → references flow to bowling pin 2 → references flow to bowling pin 3 → eventually you're across the chasm and into the early majority broadly.

The discipline:
- **Pick the first niche carefully.** It should be small enough to dominate, central enough to have adjacent niches, and exhibit clear ROI.
- **Don't broaden until you've won.** A 30% market share in one niche is more valuable than a 5% share spread across six niches — both for revenue and for the reference machine.
- **Build the whole product *for that niche* first.** Industry-specific integrations, vertical-specific support, pricing tuned to that niche.

## Inside the Tornado

Moore's sequel covers what happens after you've crossed the chasm. The tornado is when the early majority *en masse* decides the category is necessary — and the market expands explosively.

Two key dynamics in the tornado:
- **The market is buying.** You don't need to convince anyone. You need to ship. Sales cycles compress; growth becomes capacity-limited.
- **The leader takes most.** In a tornado, the company with the most momentum wins the standardization race. Second place wins much less. Third place often disappears.

The strategic implication: when you've crossed the chasm and the tornado is forming, **change your operating model.** Stop optimizing for product perfection; optimize for shipping volume. Stop bespoke selling; standardize the sales motion. *"You only have one chance to be the standard."*

Most of the journey on this site is pre-chasm. After PMF, *Inside the Tornado* becomes the relevant playbook.

## The Rachleff caveat

Moore's framework has a subtle tension with Rachleff's that's worth flagging.

Moore implicitly assumes you'd want to *cross the chasm.* Most startups should. But Rachleff is sharp:

> *"The whole product is opposite of the MVP. Stay in the MVP as long as you can. Building whole-product capabilities before the market validates value is one of the most expensive failure modes."*

Rachleff's preference: extend the early-adopter phase aggressively. Hold the product narrow. Don't build the whole product until you've maxed out the visionary segment. The temptation to cross too early — adding integrations, hiring services teams, building enterprise features — burns capital and dilutes the product.

Hold both: Moore's framework explains what you'll encounter. Rachleff's discipline keeps you from crossing too soon.

## A subtle critique

Rachleff has flagged a contradiction in Moore's book that he discussed with Moore directly. The book argues you cannot cross the chasm without a whole product. But the book also argues you should target a narrow niche first. These can be in tension: a narrow niche may not be large enough to fund the whole product build.

The resolution: **the whole product is whole-product-for-the-niche.** Not the whole-product for the entire market. Vertical integrations and industry-specific features count as "whole product" if they match the niche's actual buying criteria.

## The lifecycle's strategic implications

Knowing which psychographic you're selling to changes everything:

| If you're selling to... | Buy on... | Need... | Sales motion is... |
|---|---|---|---|
| Innovators | Novelty | Working demo | Show-and-tell |
| Early adopters | Vision | Proof of concept | Founder-led, consultative |
| Early majority | References | Whole product | Repeatable, scalable |
| Late majority | Standards | Turnkey solutions | High-volume, transactional |
| Laggards | Necessity | Replacement-only | Renewal-driven |

**A common failure mode:** founders pitch the early majority with early-adopter motion. They sell on vision to people who buy on references. The customer says "interesting" politely and never buys.

**The opposite failure mode:** founders pitch early adopters with early-majority motion. They sell on integrations and reference customers when the visionary buyer wants to hear about the breakthrough. The visionary feels they're being sold a mature product, loses interest.

Match motion to psychographic.
