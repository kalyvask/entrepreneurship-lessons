# The Cold Email Playbook

Adapted from the Lean Launchpad methodology's Week 2 lecture — *Writing Winning Cold Emails* — combined with the RDI outreach playbook. Battle-tested by founders running 100+ cold outreach campaigns.

## Why cold email works (when it works)

Most cold outreach in professional contexts is transactional — someone wants a job, a sale, an intro. Recipients are tuned to recognize and ignore this pattern within 3 seconds.

**Effective cold outreach inverts the pattern.** You're not asking for something — you're *offering* something: curiosity, a fresh perspective, the willingness to come back and share what you learn across the landscape. That's a conversation most people want to have.

**Protect that dynamic.** The moment your email starts to feel like networking or selling, you lose the thing that makes it work.

## Response rates: what's realistic

Based on observed data from teams running RDI outreach:

- **Boilerplate blast (zero personalization):** ~1–3% — not worth doing
- **Semi-tailored batched (by role/segment, name-aware):** 20–30%
- **Fully personalized (specific person, specific reference):** 60–90%+

The math: 20 personalized emails > 200 boilerplate emails.

## Anatomy of a cold email

Six elements. In order.

### 1. Sender address

This matters more than you'd think.

- `.edu` or institutional address = **huge unlock**. Carries credibility. Opens doors.
- Personal email (gmail) = much weaker default. Senders need to overcome the skepticism.
- A business domain you've registered = better than gmail but worse than .edu

If you have access to a `.edu` (current student, alum), use it for RDI outreach. Sender domain alone can multiply response rates.

### 2. Subject line

Answers two questions: *who are you, and what do you want?*

Both questions need to be implicit in the subject. The reader scans subjects in <2 seconds. They need to decide whether to open before they've opened.

**Templates that work:**

```
[Institution] [discipline] researching [specific topic]
[Institution] [discipline] researching [specific topic] — quick chat?
Researching [specific topic] — [referrer name] ref
[Referrer] suggested I reach out re: [topic]
[Specific common context] — would love a quick chat
```

Examples:

```
[School] student researching mid-market vertical farming ops
Researching CEA production planning — Sarah Chen ref
[School] alum researching DPC clinic workflows — quick chat?
```

**Templates that don't work:**

```
Quick question                          ← could be anything
Hi! I'd love your perspective           ← generic
[Your name] - introduction              ← sounds like sales
Are you the right person for this?      ← buried lede
```

### 3. Body — the five elements

The body should be **short** — under 150 words. The reader is scanning. Five elements:

**(a) Introduce yourself and your connection to the space (2–3 sentences)**

Who you are, why you're exploring this space, relevant background.

> *"I'm a [School] student exploring software opportunities in mid-market vertical farming. I'm working with a team of two; we've spent the last 3 months reading and talking to ~30 people across the space, from operators to investors to academics."*

**(b) Explain why them (1–2 sentences)**

What about their specific experience makes this conversation valuable. Show you've done your homework.

> *"Saw your recent piece in Inside CEA about labor cost dynamics in 20-50K sq ft operations — exactly the question I've been wrestling with."*

**(c) Find connections (if any)**

Mutual contacts, referrals, shared background, shared event.

> *"[Mutual contact] suggested I reach out — she mentioned you're particularly thoughtful about ops tooling."*

If a referral, name the referrer. If no referral, find another connection point: an article they wrote, a podcast they were on, a conference they attended, a school they attended.

**(d) Offer something**

What you'll share back. *Not* "let me know if I can help" (vacuous). Be specific.

> *"Happy to share what I'm learning across the industry — I've been talking to operators in 5 different segments, so I should have a useful read on the space."*

Or, more advanced:

> *"Once we've finished, we plan to share a synthesis of what we found about labor and waste dynamics across vertical farm size segments — happy to send it to you when ready."*

**(e) Clear ask and next steps**

What you want. Be specific.

> *"Would love 30 minutes if you're up for it. I can come to you for time — happy to send a Calendly link or just propose a few times."*

### 4. Signature

Brief. Affiliation. Link to anything credibility-building (LinkedIn, your work).

```
Best,
[Name]
[Year], [School]
[LinkedIn URL]
```

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Cold to a senior operator (segment they care about)

**Subject:** [School] researching mid-market vertical farming ops — quick chat?

```
Hi [Name],

I'm a [School] student exploring software opportunities for mid-market vertical farms (10–50K sq ft). My team has spent the last 3 months talking to operators, suppliers, and investors across the space — most recently [referrer] suggested I reach out.

I came across [Company]'s growth story (saw the Forbes piece) and was particularly struck by how you've scaled harvest coordination as you've added square footage. We've heard from a few other operators that this exact transition — from one site to multiple — is where existing tools break down.

Would love 30 minutes to learn from your experience. In return, I'm happy to share what we're hearing across the industry — patterns by segment, what's working / not working at different stages.

Feel free to propose a time, or here's a Calendly: [link].

Best,
Alex
1st year, [School]
[LinkedIn]
```

### Example 2: Cold to a peer at an early-stage startup

**Subject:** Researching DPC clinic workflows — would love to hear how you've thought about it

```
Hi [Name],

Saw your Series A announcement last week — congrats! I'm a [School] student exploring opportunities in direct primary care software. Specifically how DPC practices handle async patient communication (which we've found is a major source of friction in 2-5 physician practices).

You'd be the second DPC-focused founder I've spoken with, and the first who's specifically built async tooling. I'm not building anything yet — really still in research mode — but would love to learn how you've thought about this space and what surprised you.

30 minutes? I'd be happy to share patterns from across the operators I've spoken with so far.

Best,
Alex
[LinkedIn]
```

### Example 3: Warm intro

**Subject:** [Mutual contact] suggested I reach out — researching applied AI in regulated industries

```
Hi [Name],

[Mutual contact] mentioned you'd be a great person to talk to. I'm a [School] student exploring AI opportunities in regulated industries — specifically focused on healthcare and financial services compliance workflows.

She mentioned your perspective on [specific topic] would be especially valuable. Would love to learn from you.

30 minutes whenever works for you in the next 2 weeks? I'm flexible.

Best,
Alex
```

## Sourcing emails

When you don't have a referral with their email, options:

### Option 1: Email syntax inference

Find a known email format on the company's website (press releases often include media contact). Try variations:

- `firstname.lastname@company.com` (most common)
- `firstname_lastname@company.com`
- `firstnamelastname@company.com`
- `firstinitial.lastname@company.com`
- `firstinitiallastname@company.com`
- `firstname@company.com` (smaller companies)

Send to all five. The ones that bounce, you know. The one that lands is the right format. (Use a tool that doesn't notify you for bounces during the test if you want to be clean.)

### Option 2: Third-party tools

- Apollo.io
- Hunter.io
- RocketReach
- Lusha
- ZoomInfo

Mixed accuracy. Useful when other methods fail.

### Option 3: Conference attendee lists

Conference registration often gives access to attendee lists. Past attendee lists are sometimes findable with Google:

```
site:[conference website] filetype:pdf
"[conference name]" "attendee list" filetype:pdf
"[conference name]" filetype:xlsx
```

### Option 4: LinkedIn message

If email completely fails, a personalized LinkedIn message with the same five elements can work — typically with response rates 30–50% lower than email.

### Option 5: Ask whoever referred them

The single best source.

## Follow-up discipline

**A short, well-timed follow-up can double your response rate.**

After 5–7 business days of no response, send a follow-up:

```
Hi [Name],

Just wanted to bump this in case it got buried. I know inboxes are flooded.

If now's not a good time, no worries — happy to circle back in a few weeks. Or if you'd like to suggest someone else on your team who'd be a better fit, that works too.

Best,
Alex
```

**Do NOT:** send 5 follow-ups. Don't.
**Do:** one follow-up at day 5–7. If still silent, drop it. They'll come back if/when they're ready.

## Pipeline mechanics

Outreach has to be **constant and parallel.** Sourcing, emailing, following up, scheduling, interviewing, and debriefing all happen simultaneously.

**Lightweight pipeline:**
- Spreadsheet or Notion table: name, role, company, status, sourcing, when contacted, response, next action
- Status: Sourced → Reached out → Followed up → Replied → Scheduled → Done → Followed back
- Goal: ~5–10 new contacts per week, ~5–10 follow-ups per week, ~3–5 interviews per week

The pipeline self-feeds at scale: each interview produces 1–3 introductions to other people, and those introductions convert at much higher rates than cold outreach.

## Three rules that distinguish RDI outreach from sales outreach

1. **Be specific.** Generic emails get generic responses (or none). Specifics about their work, their company, your context.

2. **Offer something back.** Not "let me know if I can help" — actual value. The synthesis you'll share. The patterns across the industry. A specific introduction you can make.

3. **Don't ask for too much.** 30 minutes is the right ask. Not 60. Not a "deep dive." Not a "regular cadence." A single conversation. If it goes well, more will follow naturally.

## Common failure modes

| Failure | Fix |
|---|---|
| Boilerplate | Personalize to the specific person — reference their work |
| Too long | Keep under 150 words |
| Buried ask | Clear ask in the last 1–2 sentences |
| Vague offer | Specifically what you'll share back |
| No specificity about why them | Show you did your homework |
| Sounds salesy | Strip "synergies," "opportunities," any pitch language |
| Sending to gmail.com personal email of a senior exec | Send to corporate; use corporate domain |
| Misspelled name or title | Read aloud. Triple-check. |
| Sending Friday afternoon | Tuesday–Thursday morning has best open rates |
| Generic subject | Specific subject answers who + what |
| 5 follow-ups | Just one |

## Beyond email

Three illustrative non-email approaches from past RDI teams:
- A team researching ports hired a taco truck, parked it by the docks, gave free tacos, and asked workers about their day
- A team hosted a virtual conference on their topic, drew speakers eager for an academic audience, generated an attendee list for further outreach
- A team publicly published its learnings as they went — articles, summaries, LinkedIn posts — and inbound from execs became their most powerful channel

Common thread: **reduce friction, demonstrate genuine interest, meet people where they already are.**
